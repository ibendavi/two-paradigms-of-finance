Grullon & Michaely (2002): Dividends, Share Repurchases, and the Substitution Hypothesis

Grullon, Gustavo, and Roni Michaely. "Dividends, Share Repurchases, and the Substitution Hypothesis." *Journal of Finance* 57, no. 4 (August 2002): 1649-1684. (Grullon at Rice University; Michaely at Cornell University.)

Key Finding

Grullon & Michaely's 2002 Journal of Finance paper provides comprehensive evidence that firms have been systematically substituting share repurchases for dividends. Using Compustat data from 1972-2000 (134,646 firm-year observations), they document that while dividend payout ratios declined from ~50% to ~27% of earnings, repurchase ratios rose from 4.8% to 41.8%, keeping total payout roughly constant. The substitution is not just an aggregate trend — it operates at the firm level: firms that repurchase more have larger negative deviations from the Lintner (1956) dividend model, meaning they pay LESS in dividends than their earnings would predict. The market recognizes this substitution: firms that repurchase experience significantly smaller stock price drops when they cut dividends (CAR of −0.45% vs. −1.93% for non-repurchasers). A pivotal institutional finding is that SEC Rule 10b-18, adopted in 1982, provided safe harbor protections for repurchases and was followed by a ~1,000% increase in repurchase activity. For the EPS project, this paper is essential because share repurchases are the primary mechanism through which EPS-maximizing managers boost EPS — retiring shares when the earnings yield exceeds the after-tax borrowing cost — and Grullon & Michaely document the massive secular shift toward this tool.

Details

The Substitution Hypothesis (Section I, pp. 1649-1654)

The "substitution hypothesis" predicts that repurchases are replacing dividends as the preferred payout method. This contrasts with the "complementary hypothesis," which would have both payout forms increasing together.

Why might firms prefer repurchases?

  1. Tax advantage: repurchases generate capital gains (taxed at lower rates or deferred) rather than ordinary income
  2. Flexibility: repurchases don't create expectations of continuation (unlike dividends, which are "sticky")
  3. EPS management: repurchases reduce share count, directly boosting EPS

Regulatory context: Before SEC Rule 10b-18 (November 1982), firms faced significant legal risk from open-market repurchases — potential charges of stock manipulation under Securities Exchange Act §9(a)(2) and Rule 10b-5. Rule 10b-18 provided safe harbor conditions (volume limits, timing restrictions, single broker, price conditions) that dramatically reduced this risk.

Section II: Data (pp. 1654-1657)

  • Source: Compustat 1972-2000 (active and research files)
  • 134,646 firm-year observations
  • Repurchases measured as increase in treasury stock (Compustat item 226) when positive; alternative: cash used for purchases of common and preferred stock (item 115)
  • Dividends: common dividends (item 21)
  • Key ratios: DIV/EARN, REPO/EARN, TOTAL/EARN (total payout to earnings)

  • Dividend payers are larger, older, more profitable, and have less volatile earnings

Section IV: Firm-Level Substitution — The Lintner Test (pp. 1665-1672)

The key innovation: using the Lintner (1956) partial adjustment model to test firm-level substitution. If firms substitute repurchases for dividends, then firms that repurchase more should UNDERSHOOT their Lintner-predicted dividend levels.

Methodology:

  1. Estimate Lintner model: D_{t+1} = a₀ + a₁D_t + a₂E_{t+1}
  2. Compute forecast errors: ERROR = Actual D_{t+1} - Predicted D_{t+1}
  3. Sort firms by repurchase yield (RYIELD = repurchases/market cap)
  4. Test whether ERROR is more negative for high-RYIELD firms

Table V: Forecast errors by repurchase yield quintile

RYIELD quintile Mean ERROR
No repurchases +0.044%
Low RYIELD −0.016%
2 −0.058%
3 −0.108%
High RYIELD −0.144% to −0.326%

The pattern is strikingly monotonic: firms that repurchase more have increasingly negative dividend forecast errors. High repurchasers pay 14-33 basis points LESS in dividends than the Lintner model predicts.

Table VI: Cross-sectional regressions

ERROR_{i,t+1} = b₀ + b₁RYIELD_{i,t} + controls + ε

RYIELD coefficient: −0.01312 to −0.01766 (significant at 1% level), with controls for size, profitability, growth, industry, year. The effect is economically meaningful and survives all controls.

Section V: Market Perception of Substitution (pp. 1672-1676)

If the market understands that repurchases substitute for dividends, then dividend CUTS should be less bad news for repurchasing firms (because the market knows the cut reflects substitution, not bad prospects).

Table VII: CARs around dividend decreases

Non-repurchasing firms Repurchasing firms Difference
3-day CAR −1.93% −0.45% +1.48%

Firms that repurchase experience a 75% smaller price drop when they cut dividends. The market recognizes the substitution.

Table VIII: Multivariate regressions confirm this after controlling for size of dividend cut, firm size, profitability, and leverage. The DUMREPO coefficient (dummy for repurchasing firms) is +1.1423% (t = 2.78) — repurchasing firms experience 1.14 percentage points less negative reaction to dividend cuts.

Section VI: Rule 10b-18 and the Regulatory Catalyst (pp. 1676-1680)

Historical context:

  • Before 1982: open-market repurchases exposed firms to potential manipulation charges
  • November 1982: SEC adopted Rule 10b-18, providing safe harbor conditions:
    • Single broker per day
    • No opening or closing transactions
    • Price not exceeding highest independent bid
    • Volume ≤ 25% of average daily volume
  • Rule 10b-18 did NOT legalize repurchases (they were already legal) — it provided PROTECTION against manipulation charges

Table XI: Pre- vs. post-Rule 10b-18 repurchase levels

All measures of repurchase activity increased dramatically after 1982:

  • Mean REPO/EARN: increased ~1,000% (from negligible to substantial)
  • Mean RYIELD: similarly massive increase
  • The shift is statistically and economically overwhelming

Table IX: Tax Reform Act of 1986 and repurchase announcement CARs

The Tax Reform Act of 1986 reduced the capital gains rate from 20% to 28%, shrinking the tax advantage of repurchases relative to dividends. Consistent with the tax motive:

  • Pre-TRA 1986 repurchase announcement CAR: +3.49%
  • Post-TRA 1986 repurchase announcement CAR: +2.42%
  • Difference: −1.07% (significant)

When the tax advantage shrank, the market reacted less positively to repurchase announcements.

Table X: The TAX differential variable (dividend tax rate minus capital gains rate) is positively related to repurchase announcement CARs — wider tax wedge → more positive market reaction to repurchases.

Section VII: Conclusions (pp. 1680-1682)

"Our results strongly support the hypothesis that firms finance their share repurchase programs with funds that would otherwise be used to increase dividends."

"Perhaps most important, our results show that Rule 10b-18 may have been the single most important event in the history of corporate payout policy."

Significance

1. Repurchases Are the EPS-Maximizing Payout Tool

In the EPS paradigm, the choice between dividends and repurchases is simple: repurchases boost EPS by reducing share count, while dividends do not. Grullon & Michaely document the massive secular shift toward repurchases — exactly what the EPS paradigm predicts. As managers became increasingly focused on EPS (post-1980s), they shifted toward the payout mechanism that directly boosts EPS.

2. The Substitution Is Driven by the Same Logic as EPS Maximization

Grullon & Michaely frame the substitution as tax-driven (capital gains vs. ordinary income). The EPS paradigm offers a simpler explanation: repurchases boost EPS, dividends do not. A $1 repurchase at an earnings yield of 8% permanently boosts EPS by $0.08 per retired share; a $1 dividend has no effect on EPS. The shift from dividends to repurchases is a shift from an EPS-neutral to an EPS-accretive payout form.

3. Rule 10b-18 Enabled EPS-Maximizing Behavior

The finding that Rule 10b-18 was followed by a ~1,000% increase in repurchases is critical for the EPS paradigm's historical narrative. Before 1982, managers who wanted to boost EPS through repurchases faced legal risk. After 1982, the safe harbor removed this barrier. The timing coincides almost exactly with the rise of "EPS culture" — analyst focus on quarterly EPS, option-based compensation tied to stock price, and the buyback boom that defines modern corporate finance.

4. Stable Total Payout Supports the EPS Paradigm's Earnings-Based View

The remarkable stability of total payout (DIV + REPO ≈ 50-70% of earnings throughout 1972-2000) supports the EPS paradigm's view that payout is fundamentally about distributing earnings. The LEVEL of payout hasn't changed — only the FORM. This is consistent with managers targeting a payout ratio of earnings (as in Lintner 1956) while shifting the mix toward the form that maximizes EPS.

5. The Lintner Model Confirmation Supports Earnings-Based Payout

Grullon & Michaely use the Lintner model as their benchmark — dividends are a function of EARNINGS, with partial adjustment. The forecast errors from this earnings-based model are explained by repurchase intensity. This confirms that the underlying framework for payout is earnings-based: managers start with an earnings-determined dividend target and then reallocate some of that target to repurchases to boost EPS.

6. Market Recognizes Substitution — The EPS Signal

The finding that dividend cuts are less penalized for repurchasing firms (CAR −0.45% vs. −1.93%) has EPS implications. In the EPS paradigm, the market cares about EPS, not dividends per se. If a firm cuts dividends but uses the saved cash for repurchases, EPS goes UP (fewer shares). The market penalizes the dividend cut less because EPS is maintained or improved. The market is pricing the EPS trajectory, not the dividend trajectory.

7. New Firms Choose Repurchases — Generational Shift in EPS Culture

The finding that >84% of new payout initiators choose repurchases (up from <27%) reflects a generational shift in corporate culture toward EPS maximization. New firms, managed by a generation raised on EPS targets and option compensation, naturally choose the EPS-boosting payout form. They are not "deciding" between dividends and repurchases based on tax optimization — they are choosing the tool that directly serves their EPS objective.

8. Timing Aligns with the EPS-Financial Markets Nexus

The repurchase boom documented by Grullon & Michaely (concentrated in the 1990s-2000s) coincides exactly with the period analyzed in the Ben-David, Chinco, Meli paper on EPS maximization and financial markets. The falling interest rates of the 1990s-2000s made debt-financed buybacks increasingly EPS-accretive, amplifying the substitution trend.

Connections

  • Lintner (1956) — "Distribution of Incomes of Corporations Among Dividends, Retained Earnings, and Taxes." Grullon & Michaely use Lintner's partial adjustment model as the benchmark for expected dividends. Firms that repurchase deviate from Lintner's predictions.
  • Fama & French (2001) — "Disappearing Dividends." Document the decline in dividend-paying firms. Grullon & Michaely show that total payout hasn't disappeared — it has shifted form.
  • Fama & French (2002) — "Testing Trade-Off and Pecking Order Predictions about Dividends and Debt." Confirm Lintner model with SOA ≈ 0.30 and TP ≈ 41-46%. Grullon & Michaely show deviations from these predictions are driven by repurchase substitution.
  • Ikenberry, Lakonishok & Vermaelen (1995) — "Market Underreaction to Open Market Share Repurchases." Document positive long-run abnormal returns following repurchase announcements. Grullon & Michaely provide the context: repurchases are substituting for dividends, not just opportunistic buying.
  • Jagannathan, Stephens & Weisbach (2000) — "Financial Flexibility and the Choice Between Dividends and Stock Repurchases." Argue dividends are paid from permanent cash flows, repurchases from temporary. Grullon & Michaely's substitution hypothesis challenges this: repurchases are replacing dividends even for stable payers.
  • Allen & Michaely (2003) — "Payout Policy." Handbook chapter surveying the payout literature. Grullon & Michaely's substitution finding is a central result.
  • DeAngelo, DeAngelo & Skinner (2004) — "Are Dividends Disappearing? Dividend Concentration and the Consolidation of Earnings." Show dividends are concentrating among large firms, not disappearing. Consistent with Grullon & Michaely: small firms substitute repurchases; large firms maintain dividends.
  • Brav, Graham, Harvey & Michaely (2005) — "Payout Policy in the 21st Century." Survey evidence that managers prefer repurchases for flexibility and view dividends as commitments. Consistent with Grullon & Michaely's substitution finding.
  • Skinner (2008) — "The Evolving Relation Between Earnings, Dividends, and Stock Repurchases." Shows repurchases track earnings more closely than dividends do. Extends Grullon & Michaely's analysis.
  • DeAngelo & DeAngelo (2006) — "The Irrelevance of the MM Dividend Irrelevance Theorem." MM's framework assumes 100% FCF payout, eliminating the retention choice. Grullon & Michaely show the payout FORM choice (dividends vs. repurchases) is a real and consequential decision.
  • Grullon, Michaely & Swaminathan (2002) — "Are Dividend Changes a Sign of Firm Maturity?" Companion paper. Dividend changes signal maturity transitions, consistent with the shift from dividends to repurchases as firms evolve.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS) — The substitution from dividends to repurchases is the EPS paradigm's most direct prediction about payout: repurchases boost EPS (retire shares at the earnings yield), dividends do not. The secular shift toward repurchases is the secular shift toward explicit EPS maximization.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS and dividends) — Grullon & Michaely's substitution evidence is the primary motivation: the EPS-maximizing payout paper explains WHY firms choose repurchases over dividends (EPS accretion) and WHEN they do so (when earnings yield exceeds after-tax borrowing cost).
  • Ben-David & Chinco, Meli (EPS and financial markets) — The timing of the repurchase boom (1990s-2000s) coincides with falling rates making debt-financed buybacks increasingly EPS-accretive. Grullon & Michaely document the payout transformation; the Meli paper explains the macro driver.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (maxEPS paradigm) — Rule 10b-18 (1982) is a key institutional event in the maxEPS paradigm's historical narrative. Before 10b-18, EPS maximization through repurchases was legally risky. After 10b-18, the primary tool of EPS maximization was unlocked — and repurchases exploded.
