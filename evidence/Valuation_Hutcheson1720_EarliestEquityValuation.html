Key Finding

Archibald Hutcheson's 1720 pamphlets contain the earliest known systematic equity valuation exercises, computing "intrinsick value" of South Sea Company stock. His method is earnings capitalization: take the Company's annuity income, capitalize it at a market rate (V = income/r), subtract liabilities, divide by shares outstanding. Details

Valuation Method: Earnings Capitalization

Hutcheson capitalizes the Company's income stream at a required rate of return and divides by shares outstanding. The Company's main "asset" is a stream of government annuity payments â€” but valuing that stream at income/r IS earnings capitalization. The fact that he frames it as "valuing assets" does not change what the math is: V = E/r.

The accounting wrapper (assets minus liabilities, divided by shares) is just the vehicle. The valuation step â€” the step where a number gets turned into a price â€” is capitalization of income at a market rate.

The "Intrinsick Value" Concept

Hutcheson uses "intrinsick value" to mean: what the stock is worth based on the Company's income, capitalized at a reasonable rate. He compares this to the market price and shows they have diverged enormously. This is the same concept as Graham & Dodd's "intrinsic value" â€” a fundamental value derived from earnings and a capitalization rate, compared to market price to identify over/undervaluation.

Sophistication Level

  • Earnings capitalization (V = income/r): Yes â€” this IS what the numbers show
  • Discount rate sensitivity: Yes (shows values at 3%, 4%, 5%, 6%)
  • Dilution from new issuances: Yes
  • Sustainability of dividends: Yes
  • Finite-horizon PV for terminable annuities: Yes
  • Trading/operating income projection: Basic (rough estimate of trade profits)
  • Risk adjustment: No â€” uses risk-free government rates as cap rates
  • Market efficiency concept: Implicit â€” argues market is wrong, intrinsic value differs from price

Significance

  1. Earliest known systematic equity valuation: Predates all other known formal equity valuation exercises. Uses earnings capitalization â€” the same approach that would dominate practitioner valuation for the next 300 years.
  2. Practitioner, not academic: Real-time financial analysis by a Member of Parliament trying to warn investors â€” pure practitioner stream.
  3. The multiples/capitalization approach is ancient: Hutcheson's method â€” capitalize income at a market rate, compare to price â€” is exactly what practitioners still do today (P = EPS x P/E, or equivalently, V = E / cap rate). The method long predates its formalization in textbooks.
  4. "Intrinsick value" vs. market price: The earliest known use of fundamental valuation to identify a bubble. Hutcheson shows the stock is 3-5x overvalued by any reasonable capitalization rate.
  5. The finite-horizon PV is a refinement, not the core method: For terminable annuities, Hutcheson correctly uses finite-horizon present value (bond math from de Witt 1671). But the conceptual framework is still capitalization: what is this income stream worth at a given rate? The PV calculation is a tool within the capitalization framework, not a separate "DCF" approach to equity.
