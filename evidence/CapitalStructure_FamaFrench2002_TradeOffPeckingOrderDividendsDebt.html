Fama & French (2002): Testing Trade-Off and Pecking Order Predictions about Dividends and Debt

Fama, Eugene F., and Kenneth R. French. "Testing Trade-Off and Pecking Order Predictions about Dividends and Debt." *Review of Financial Studies* 15, no. 1 (Spring 2002): 1-33. (Fama at University of Chicago; French at MIT Sloan.)

Key Finding

Fama & French's 2002 Review of Financial Studies paper jointly tests the trade-off and pecking order models' predictions about dividends and leverage using Fama-MacBeth cross-sectional regressions on Compustat data from 1965-1999. Many predictions of the two models overlap and are confirmed: more profitable firms pay higher dividends, firms with more investments have lower payouts, and short-term variation in earnings and investment is largely absorbed by debt (not equity). The target dividend payout ratio is approximately 41-46%, with a slow speed of adjustment (27-33% per year, consistent with Lintner 1956). On leverage, the models disagree in two important ways: (1) more profitable firms have LESS book leverage ‚Äî supporting pecking order but contradicting the trade-off model's prediction that profitable firms should have more leverage (this is the "important failure" of the trade-off model); and (2) leverage is mean-reverting, but at a "suspiciously slow" rate of 7-17% per year ‚Äî ambiguous between genuine targeting (trade-off) and spurious mean reversion from autocorrelated cash flows (pecking order). The paper also documents that less-levered nonpayers are typically small growth firms that issue large amounts of equity despite having low-risk debt capacity ‚Äî a "deep wound" on the pecking order. For the EPS project, this paper is essential because: (1) the finding that more profitable firms have LESS leverage is exactly what the EPS paradigm predicts (profitable firms have low earnings yields, making equity "cheap" relative to debt, so they have less incentive to lever up); (2) the Lintner (1956) dividend smoothing model is confirmed, supporting the earnings-based view of payout; and (3) the paper documents that debt absorbs short-term variation in earnings and investment, consistent with the EPS paradigm's view of debt as a tool for managing EPS.

Details

Predictions of the Two Models (Section 1, pp. 1-8)

Pecking order model (Myers 1984):

  • Firms prefer internal financing (retained earnings), then debt, then equity ‚Äî due to asymmetric information costs
  • More profitable firms ‚Üí higher dividends (more internal funds available) and LESS leverage (less need for external finance)
  • Dividends are "sticky" (for unknown reasons), so short-term variation in earnings and investment is absorbed by debt
  • Simple version: more investment ‚Üí more book leverage (debt finances investment)
  • Complex version: more investment ‚Üí less leverage (firms keep debt capacity in reserve)

Trade-off model:

  • Firms balance tax benefits of debt against bankruptcy costs, agency costs, and adjustment costs
  • More profitable firms ‚Üí higher dividends (agency costs of FCF) and MORE leverage (higher taxable income ‚Üí more tax shield value)
  • Target leverage exists; firms adjust toward it over time
  • Firms with more nondebt tax shields (depreciation, R&D) ‚Üí less leverage
  • Firms with less volatile earnings ‚Üí more leverage (lower bankruptcy risk)

Key overlap: Both models predict that more profitable firms pay higher dividends, and firms with more investments pay lower dividends. Both predict a negative relation between the target payout ratio and leverage. The models diverge sharply on the LEVERAGE-PROFITABILITY relation.

Significance

1. The Profitability-Leverage Puzzle Is Natural in the EPS Paradigm

The paper's most important finding is the "important failure" of the trade-off model: more profitable firms have LESS leverage. This is the central puzzle of capital structure that has bedeviled the field since at least Long & Malitz (1985) and Rajan & Zingales (1995). In the EPS paradigm, it is no puzzle at all: profitable firms have HIGH EPS and thus LOW earnings yields (high P/E). A low earnings yield means equity is "cheap" relative to debt ‚Äî the cost of equity (the earnings yield) is low, so there is little incentive to substitute debt for equity to boost EPS. Less profitable firms have HIGH earnings yields, making equity "expensive," giving them strong incentives to lever up and boost EPS by replacing expensive equity with cheaper debt.

2. Lintner's Dividend Model Is Confirmed ‚Äî Earnings Drive Payouts

The confirmation of Lintner (1956) ‚Äî target payout ‚âà 41-46% of earnings, SOA ‚âà 0.30 ‚Äî supports the EPS paradigm's view that earnings are the fundamental quantity managers manage. Managers set dividends as a fraction of EARNINGS (not cash flows, not firm value), and they smooth dividends relative to EARNINGS. The entire dividend framework is earnings-based, exactly as the EPS paradigm would predict.

3. Debt Absorbs Short-Term Variation ‚Äî The EPS Buffer

The finding that "short-term variation in earnings and investment is largely absorbed by debt" is precisely what the EPS paradigm predicts. Managers smooth EPS by using debt as a buffer: when earnings drop temporarily, they borrow rather than cut dividends or reduce investment (which would signal EPS weakness). When investment spikes temporarily, they borrow rather than issue equity (which would dilute EPS). Debt is the residual in the EPS paradigm ‚Äî it adjusts to maintain the earnings-per-share trajectory.

4. The Speed of Adjustment Is Consistent with EPS Targeting

The slow speed of leverage adjustment (7-17% per year) is puzzling for the trade-off model (why don't firms adjust faster to capture tax benefits?). In the EPS paradigm, the speed is governed by how quickly the earnings yield and borrowing rates change. Since these move slowly, leverage adjusts slowly. There is no "puzzle" ‚Äî leverage changes when the relative cost of debt vs. equity (measured by the earnings yield) changes, and this happens gradually.

5. Growth Firms Issue Equity Because Their Earnings Yield Is Low

The pecking order's "deep wound" ‚Äî small growth firms issuing lots of equity ‚Äî is natural in the EPS paradigm. Growth firms have HIGH P/E ratios (LOW earnings yields). A low earnings yield means equity is cheap relative to debt. For these firms, issuing equity does NOT significantly dilute EPS because the share price is high relative to earnings. They issue equity precisely because it is their cheapest source of capital, measured by the earnings yield. No information asymmetry story is needed.

6. The Trade-Off vs. Pecking Order Debate Is Dissolved by EPS

The entire trade-off vs. pecking order debate assumes managers maximize firm value subject to various frictions (taxes, bankruptcy costs, information asymmetry). The EPS paradigm dissolves this debate: managers maximize EPS, and the leverage-profitability pattern follows directly from the earnings yield being the cost of equity. No frictions are needed. The "failures" of both models ‚Äî the profitability-leverage puzzle for trade-off, the equity issuance of growth firms for pecking order ‚Äî are not puzzles in the EPS framework.
