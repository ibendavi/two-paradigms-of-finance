De Bondt & Thaler (1985): Does the Stock Market Overreact?

De Bondt, Werner F. M., and Richard Thaler. "Does the Stock Market Overreact?" *Journal of Finance* 40, no. 3 (July 1985): 793-805. (De Bondt at Cornell University; Thaler at Cornell University. Presidential Address session paper.)

Key Finding

De Bondt & Thaler's 1985 Journal of Finance paper provides the first systematic evidence that stock prices overreact to information. Using NYSE stocks from 1926-1982, they form portfolios of extreme past "winners" (top 35 stocks by 3-year cumulative excess returns) and "losers" (bottom 35) and track subsequent performance. Losers outperform winners by 24.6% (t = 2.20) over the following 36 months. The effect is strongly asymmetric: losers gain 19.6% in abnormal returns while winners lose only 5.0%. Much of the loser effect is concentrated in January. Critically, loser portfolios have LOWER betas than winner portfolios (1.026 vs. 1.369), so the reversal cannot be explained by risk compensation. The authors motivate the finding with Kahneman & Tversky's representativeness heuristic â€” investors overweight recent information and extrapolate trends â€” creating predictable reversals. For the EPS project, this paper connects directly to the P/E anomaly: "losers" are low P/E stocks (earnings yield is high relative to depressed price), and "winners" are high P/E stocks (earnings yield is low relative to inflated price). The overreaction-driven reversal is the mechanism by which extreme P/E ratios revert to normal, generating the value premium.

Details

Introduction and Hypotheses (pp. 793-795)

Motivation from psychology:

The paper opens by connecting to Kahneman & Tversky's (1982) work on "intuitive prediction." People overreact to dramatic, salient information and underweight base rates. Applied to stock markets:

"If stock prices systematically overshoot, then their reversal should be predictable from past return data alone, with no use of any accounting data such as earnings."

Two hypotheses:

  1. Extreme movements in stock prices will be followed by subsequent price movements in the opposite direction
  2. The more extreme the initial price movement, the greater will be the subsequent adjustment

These hypotheses directly contradict the weak form of the efficient markets hypothesis, which says past returns should not predict future returns.

Methodology (pp. 795-797)

Data: Monthly returns for all NYSE stocks on CRSP, January 1926 - December 1982.

Portfolio formation:

  • At the end of each December, compute cumulative excess returns for each stock over the prior 3 years (the "formation period")
  • Rank all stocks; form "winner" portfolio (top 35 stocks) and "loser" portfolio (bottom 35 stocks)
  • Track both portfolios over the next 3 years (the "test period")
  • Repeat: 16 non-overlapping 3-year formation/test cycles from 1933-1980

Performance metric: Cumulative average residual (CAR) â€” the average cumulative market-adjusted return for the portfolio. Market adjustment is the equally weighted NYSE index.

The loser portfolio dramatically outperforms the winner portfolio. The effect builds gradually over 36 months, with most of the divergence occurring in months 13-36.

Strong asymmetry: The loser effect (19.6%) is roughly four times the winner effect (5.0%). Prior losers gain substantially more than prior winners lose. This asymmetry is consistent with the idea that investors overreact more to bad news than to good news â€” or that extreme price declines create larger mispricings than extreme price increases.

The longer the formation period, the larger the subsequent reversal â€” confirming Hypothesis 2 that more extreme movements generate larger corrections. Even 1-year formation periods produce significant reversals.

January Seasonality (pp. 799-801)

A large fraction of the excess returns for loser portfolios is earned in January â€” particularly the January of each test year. The January effect is concentrated in the loser portfolio: winners show no particular January seasonality.

"Most of the excess returns are realized in January."

De Bondt & Thaler link this to the tax-loss selling hypothesis: losers are disproportionately sold in December for tax reasons, depressing their prices further, then rebound in January. However, they argue tax-loss selling cannot fully explain the overreaction effect because: (1) the reversal persists across all months (not just January), (2) the effect exists even for formation periods that don't include year-end, and (3) the magnitude is too large for tax-motivated selling to explain.

Risk: The CAPM Beta Argument (pp. 801-802)

Critical finding â€” losers are LESS risky than winners:

If the loser-winner spread were compensation for risk, losers should have higher betas. But the data show the opposite:

Portfolio Average beta
Loser 1.026
Winner 1.369

Losers have beta 1.026 (slightly above market) while winners have beta 1.369 (well above market). The risk-based explanation goes in the wrong direction: winners are riskier but earn lower returns, losers are safer but earn higher returns.

Using the Sharpe-Lintner CAPM to adjust for risk INCREASES the loser-winner spread from 24.6% to approximately 32% over 36 months.

Connection to P/E and Accounting Variables (pp. 803-804)

This section is important for the EPS paradigm.

De Bondt & Thaler explicitly connect their findings to the P/E anomaly:

"There is also a clear connection between the overreaction hypothesis and what is sometimes called the P/E effect: extreme losers... tend to have low P/E ratios (since their earnings are low relative to what they once were). The overreaction hypothesis predicts that these stocks will earn above-normal returns."

They note that Basu (1977) documented that low P/E stocks outperform high P/E stocks, and that this finding can be understood as a manifestation of overreaction: stocks with low P/Es are prior losers whose prices have overshot to the downside.

They also reference Graham (1959) who had noted that "the market always over-values stocks that show an excellent growth record and under-values companies in difficulties." This is essentially the overreaction hypothesis expressed in practitioner language decades earlier.

Conclusions (pp. 804-805)

"The main conclusion of this paper is that the stock market appears to overreact."

The authors acknowledge that their findings are "consistent with the predictions of the overreaction hypothesis" but cannot rule out all alternative explanations. They note the findings challenge the efficient market hypothesis and suggest that contrarian strategies â€” buying losers, selling winners â€” can generate abnormal returns.

Significance

1. Overreaction IS P/E Mean Reversion

De Bondt & Thaler's "overreaction" is, in the EPS paradigm's language, P/E mean reversion. Extreme losers have collapsed prices but relatively stable earnings, producing very LOW P/E ratios (high earnings yields). Extreme winners have inflated prices relative to earnings, producing very HIGH P/E ratios (low earnings yields). The subsequent "reversal" is simply P/E ratios reverting toward normal levels. In P = EPS x P/E, the overreaction occurs in the P/E component, and the reversal is the correction of the P/E component.

2. Loser Asymmetry Reflects Earnings Yield Mechanics

The strong asymmetry â€” losers gain 19.6% but winners lose only 5.0% â€” is consistent with the EPS paradigm. A stock that drops 80% in price (to a very low P/E) needs only a modest P/E expansion to gain substantially. But a stock that rises 80% (to a very high P/E) needs only a small P/E contraction to lose a modest amount. The asymmetry in returns reflects the convexity of the P/E-to-price relationship.

3. January Effect Is Earnings-Related

Much of the loser outperformance comes in January. In the EPS paradigm, the turn of the year brings new analyst forecasts for next-12-month EPS. If losers' EPS forecasts stabilize or improve (because earnings are mean-reverting), the market reprices these stocks using updated EPS forecasts Ã— comps-based P/E. The January effect may reflect the annual repricing of EPS expectations for beaten-down stocks.

4. Risk Goes the Wrong Way â€” Consistent with EPS Paradigm

Losers have LOWER betas than winners, meaning the reversal cannot be risk compensation. In the EPS paradigm, this is expected: prior losers have low earnings yields (high P/E... wait, no) â€” actually, prior losers have HIGH earnings yields (low P/E) because price has fallen more than earnings. High earnings yield stocks are "cheap" in the EPS paradigm and should outperform as P/E reverts. The CAPM has nothing to say about this mechanism.

5. Graham Anticipated This in Practitioner Language

De Bondt & Thaler cite Graham (1959): the market "always over-values stocks that show an excellent growth record and under-values companies in difficulties." Graham was an earnings-capitalization practitioner. His entire methodology â€” buy below "intrinsic value" based on earnings power, sell when price exceeds fair value â€” is the EPS paradigm in action. The overreaction effect is what creates the opportunity Graham's methodology exploits.

6. This Is the Mirror Image of Momentum (Jegadeesh & Titman 1993)

De Bondt & Thaler find reversals at 3-5 year horizons. Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) find momentum at 3-12 month horizons. Together they describe the full lifecycle of price adjustment in the EPS paradigm: (1) an earnings surprise moves the P/E multiple, (2) the P/E overshoots in the direction of the surprise (momentum phase, 3-12 months), (3) the P/E reverts toward equilibrium (reversal phase, 1-5 years). The EPS paradigm explains both effects through the dynamics of the P/E multiple.

7. The "Overreaction" Is Not Irrational â€” It's Comps-Based Pricing

De Bondt & Thaler frame their finding as evidence of investor irrationality (via Kahneman & Tversky). The EPS paradigm offers a non-behavioral alternative: investors use comps to set P/E multiples. When a stock drops sharply, its P/E falls because peer-comparison metrics show it's now "cheap." But comps are backward-looking â€” the stock's new low P/E gradually attracts buyers who recognize the earnings yield is high, slowly pushing the P/E back up. The "overreaction" is not irrational â€” it's the natural dynamics of comps-based pricing where recent price changes temporarily push P/E away from its comps-implied level.
