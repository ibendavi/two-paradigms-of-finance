Miller & Modigliani (1961): Dividend Policy, Growth, and the Valuation of Shares

Miller, Merton H., and Franco Modigliani. "Dividend Policy, Growth, and the Valuation of Shares." *Journal of Business* 34, no. 4 (October 1961): 411-433. (Miller was Professor of Finance, University of Chicago; Modigliani was Professor of Economics, Northwestern University.)

Key Finding

Miller & Modigliani's 1961 Journal of Business paper proves that, under perfect capital markets, rational behavior, and perfect certainty, dividend policy has no effect on the current price of shares or on the total return to shareholders. The proof is elegant: when a firm pays a higher dividend while holding its investment policy fixed, it must raise more external capital (by issuing new shares), and the dilution to existing shareholders exactly offsets the extra dividend. The paper's deeper contribution is presenting four formally equivalent valuation approaches ‚Äî discounted cash flow, current earnings plus investment opportunities, stream of dividends, and stream of earnings ‚Äî and showing they are different representations of the same underlying model. The constant growth specialization yields V(0) = X(0)(1-k)/(œÅ - kœÅ*), which reduces to V(0) = X(0)/œÅ when there are no growth opportunities (œÅ* = œÅ). The paper explicitly critiques the "stream of earnings" approach as prone to misinterpretation, coins the concept of "informational content" of dividends, introduces the "clientele effect," and extends irrelevance to uncertainty via "symmetric market rationality." For the EPS project, this is the second MM pillar (after MM 1958): it establishes dividend irrelevance and, in doing so, explicitly grapples with the earnings-based valuation approach that the EPS paradigm revives.

Details

Section I: Effect of Dividend Policy with Perfect Markets, Rational Behavior, and Perfect Certainty (pp. 411-415)

Three assumptions:

  1. "Perfect capital markets" ‚Äî no buyer/seller large enough to affect prices, equal and costless access to information, no transaction costs, no tax differentials between dividends and capital gains
  2. "Rational behavior" ‚Äî investors prefer more wealth to less, are indifferent between dividends and capital gains of equal value
  3. "Perfect certainty" ‚Äî complete assurance about future investment programs and future profits

The fundamental principle of valuation (Eq. 1):

[d_j(t) + p_j(t+1) - p_j(t)] / p_j(t) = œÅ(t), independent of j

The rate of return (dividends + capital gains, divided by price) is the same for all shares in each period.

Equivalently (Eq. 2):

p_j(t) = 1/(1+œÅ) √ó [d_j(t) + p_j(t+1)]

Restating in terms of the total value of the enterprise (Eq. 3):

V(t) = 1/(1+œÅ) √ó [D(t) + V(t+1) - m(t+1)p(t+1)]

where D(t) is total dividends, V(t+1) is next period's total value, and m(t+1)p(t+1) is the value of newly issued shares.

The irrelevance proof (Eq. 5):

The amount of new capital raised externally must be (Eq. 4):

m(t+1)p(t+1) = I(t) - [X(t) - D(t)]

Substituting into (3):

V(t) = 1/(1+œÅ) √ó [X(t) - I(t) + V(t+1)]

Since D(t) does not appear in this expression, and since X(t), I(t), œÅ(t), and V(t+1) are all independent of D(t) by assumption, the current value of the firm is independent of the current dividend decision.

"Once you think of it, it is, after all, merely one more instance of the general principle that there are no 'financial illusions' in a rational and perfect economic environment. Values there are determined solely by 'real' considerations ‚Äî in this case the earning power of the firm's assets and its investment policy ‚Äî and not by how the fruits of the earning power are 'packaged' for distribution."

But they immediately warn against the "straw man" version ‚Äî defining value as simply the discounted sum of future total earnings:

"In fairness, we should point out that there is no one, to our knowledge, who has seriously advanced this view."

They also note that others have taken the "supposed earnings counter-view to the dividend approach" as "simply the proposition that price is proportional to current earnings, i.e., V(0) = X(0)/œÅ." MM say this IS valid when œÅ* = œÅ, but not in general.

Section IV: Effects of Dividend Policy Under Uncertainty (pp. 426-430)

MM extend dividend irrelevance to uncertainty by introducing two concepts:

"Imputed rationality" ‚Äî an individual trader "imputes rationality to the market" if, in forming expectations, he assumes every other trader is rational.

"Symmetric market rationality" ‚Äî the market as a whole satisfies this if every trader both is rational and imputes rationality to all others. This rules out "speculative bubbles" where an investor buys an overpriced security expecting to resell before the bubble bursts.

The irrelevance proof under uncertainty (pp. 428-429): Consider two firms with identical future earnings streams XÃÉ(t) and investment streams ƒ®(t) but different current dividends. By symmetric market rationality, investors know that any difference in current dividends must be offset by an opposite difference in dilution from new share issuance. Therefore ·πº‚ÇÅ(1) = ·πº‚ÇÇ(1), and hence RÃÉ‚ÇÅ(0) = RÃÉ‚ÇÇ(0), so V‚ÇÅ(0) = V‚ÇÇ(0).

Dividend policy and leverage (p. 429-430): The proof extends when V is interpreted as total market value (equity + debt). The accounting identity broadens to include interest on debt, and the conclusion remains: value is independent of dividend policy given investment policy.

The informational content of dividends (p. 430): MM coin this important concept to resolve the apparent contradiction between irrelevance and the observed phenomenon that dividend changes are followed by price changes:

"Where a firm has adopted a policy of dividend stabilization with a long-established and generally appreciated 'target payout ratio,' investors are likely to (and have good reason to) interpret a change in the dividend rate as a change in management's views of future profit prospects for the firm."

The dividend CHANGE provides information about EARNINGS; it is not the dividend LEVEL that affects value. This is "informational content" ‚Äî dividends signal, they don't cause.

Section V: Dividend Policy and Market Imperfections (pp. 431-433)

Taxes: Capital gains are taxed at lower rates than dividends ‚Üí systematic preference for low-payout firms. But:

  • The "clientele effect" ‚Äî each firm attracts investors suited to its payout ratio
  • Tax-exempt institutions hold a substantial fraction of shares
  • "Except for taxable individuals in the very top brackets, the required difference in before-tax yields to produce equal after-tax yields is not particularly striking"

The "only" systematic imperfection (p. 432): If the tax advantage of capital gains is the major market imperfection, then low-payout stocks should sell at a PREMIUM. If they instead sell at a DISCOUNT (as the conventional view holds), "there would be only one way to account for it; namely, as the result of systematic irrationality on the part of the investing public."

Footnote 34 on p. 432: MM suggest the alternative explanation is "less plausibly, that there is a systematic tendency for external funds to be used more productively than internal funds." This foreshadows Jensen's (1986) free cash flow theory.

Significance

1. V(0) = X(0)/œÅ Is the EPS Paradigm's Pricing Equation

MM's Eq. 12 decomposes firm value into X(0)/œÅ (capitalized current earnings) plus the NPV of growth opportunities. When œÅ* = œÅ, the growth term vanishes and V = X(0)/œÅ ‚Äî exactly P = EPS √ó P/E with P/E = 1/œÅ. MM treat this as a "special case"; the EPS paradigm treats it as the GENERAL case that practitioners actually use. The disagreement is empirical: how large is the growth opportunities term in practice, and does the market actually compute it?

2. The Four Equivalent Valuation Approaches All Include an Earnings Approach

MM explicitly validate the earnings approach (Approach 4, Eq. 18-20) as "meaningful and consistent." They warn against the straw man of "simply discounting total earnings" ‚Äî but they acknowledge that V = X(0)/œÅ is correct when there are no abnormal growth opportunities. The EPS paradigm's claim is not the straw man that MM demolish, but the valid special case that MM themselves derive.

3. Dividend Irrelevance Is Compatible with the EPS Paradigm

MM show that dividends don't affect value. The EPS paradigm doesn't claim that dividends affect value ‚Äî it claims that managers prefer REPURCHASES over dividends because repurchases boost EPS (by reducing share count) while dividends do not. The EPS paradigm's payout predictions are fully consistent with MM's irrelevance proposition: payout form matters for EPS, even though total payout is irrelevant for value.

4. The Growth Model Explains Cross-Sectional P/E Variation

V(0) = X(0)(1-k)/(œÅ-kœÅ*) shows that the P/E ratio depends on the retention ratio (k), the return on investment (œÅ*), and the market capitalization rate (œÅ). In the EPS paradigm, P/E is determined by comps ‚Äî but the COMPS THEMSELVES reflect these same fundamentals. High-growth industries have high P/E comps because their constituent firms have high œÅ*. The EPS paradigm and MM agree on what determines P/E cross-sectionally; they disagree on whether individual investors explicitly compute it or take it from comps.

5. "Informational Content" Supports Earnings-Based Pricing

MM's concept of "informational content" is that dividend changes signal EARNINGS changes. The market reacts to dividends because dividends carry information about earnings, not because dividends have value in themselves. This is exactly the EPS paradigm's view: the market prices EARNINGS. Dividends are relevant only as signals about the earnings trajectory. The informational content concept implicitly concedes that earnings are what the market "really" capitalizes.

6. The Internal Financing Special Case Creates the Illusion of Optimal Dividends

MM show that when all financing is internal, dividend policy and investment policy are indistinguishable, creating the illusion of an optimal dividend policy. They warn this case has been "treacherous" for researchers who mistake it for the general case. In the EPS paradigm, the internal financing case IS approximately the general case: most firms finance primarily from internal funds (Donaldson 1961, Myers 1984), so dividend policy IS de facto investment policy. The "treacherous" special case may be the empirically relevant one.

7. The Clientele Effect Supports EPS-Based Payout Choice

MM's clientele effect ‚Äî each firm attracts investors suited to its payout ratio ‚Äî has an EPS analog: each firm attracts investors who value its EPS growth trajectory. Firms that maximize EPS through repurchases attract "growth" investors; firms that pay dividends attract "income" investors. The clientele self-selects based on the EPS characteristics of the firm.

8. MM Dismiss the Observed Dividend Premium as "Irrationality"

If low-payout stocks sell at a discount despite tax advantages, MM say the only explanation is "systematic irrationality." The EPS paradigm offers a different explanation: high-payout (high dividend) firms may have higher P/E ratios because the market values the STABILITY implied by steady dividends ‚Äî dividends signal earnings persistence, and persistent earnings command higher multiples (Collins & Kothari 1989).

9. The Paper Establishes the Analytical Framework for All Payout Research

Like MM (1958) for capital structure, MM (1961) is the starting point for all subsequent payout research: signaling theories (Bhattacharya 1979), agency theories (Easterbrook 1984, Jensen 1986), clientele theories (Allen, Bernardo & Welch 2000), catering theories (Baker & Wurgler 2004). Each adds a friction to explain why dividends might matter. The EPS paradigm explains payout choices without frictions: managers choose the payout form (repurchases > dividends) that maximizes EPS.

Paradigm

Practitioner

Earnings capitalization: P = EPS √ó P/E from comps. Cost of equity = earnings yield. Objective = maximize EPS.

Era

The Revolution (1958‚Äì1963)

In just five years, Modigliani-Miller, Markowitz, Sharpe, and Solomon replaced earnings maximization with value maximization as the academic standard.
