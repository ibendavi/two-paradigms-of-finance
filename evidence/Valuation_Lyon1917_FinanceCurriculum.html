Lyon/IBA (1917): Courses of Study in Corporation Finance and Investment

Lyon, Hastings. *Courses of Study in Corporation Finance and Investment.* Published by Doubleday, Page & Co. for the Investment Bankers Association of America, 1917. Education Committee: Lawrence Chamberlain (chair).

Key Finding

This 1917 curriculum guide from the Investment Bankers Association reveals the state of finance education at the moment it was transitioning from pure practitioner craft to academic discipline. Key revelation: valuation in 1917 meant credit analysis and safety-margin analysis, NOT present-value calculation. Bond yield math existed, but applying discounting to equity was absent. There was no theory of asset pricing, no risk-return framework, no portfolio theory. Finance was institutional-descriptive, not theoretical.

Details

Two Full Courses

  1. Corporation Finance (15 topics): Legal form, promotion, securities instruments, capitalization, surplus/dividends, financial statements, expansion, regulation
  2. Investment (30 topics): Bond valuation (basis/yield), government/municipal/corporate bonds, stock as investment, mathematics of investment, fiduciary rules, fraud detection

How Stock Was "Valued" (Topic XVII)

Students analyzed stocks via:

  • Price in relation to dividends (dividend yield)
  • Earnings available for dividends (coverage)
  • Accumulated surplus on the stock
  • Percentage of net earnings consumed in fixed charges
  • "Income return to the purchaser"

This is a dividend-and-earnings-coverage framework — no DCF, no intrinsic value calculation, no present value applied to equity.

What Was NOT Taught

  • No equilibrium pricing theory
  • No risk-return tradeoff (formal)
  • No portfolio theory (though diversification mentioned qualitatively: spread across investments, enterprises, geography, maturities)
  • No efficient markets concept
  • No time value of money applied to equity (only to bonds)
  • No Irving Fisher influence visible

Key Textbooks Required

  • Chamberlain, Lawrence — Principles of Bond Investment (1911) — the dominant text
  • Lyon, Hastings — Corporation Finance (1916) — primary corp fin text
  • Gerstenberg, C. W. — Materials of Corporation Finance (1915)
  • Sprague, C. E. — Accountancy of Investment (1914) — bond math
  • Collver, Clinton — How to Analyze Industrial Securities (1917, Moody's)

Investment vs. Speculation

Framed as a spectrum, not binary: stocks involve "a combination of speculation and investment" — both expected income and anticipated appreciation. Remarkably sophisticated for 1917.

Proto-Diversification (Topic XXVIII)

Four principles: (1) limit proportion in any one investment, (2) limit in any one kind of enterprise, (3) distribute geographically, (4) vary maturity dates. Pure intuition, 35 years before Markowitz formalized it.

Industry-Driven, Not Academic

  • Commissioned by investment bankers, not universities
  • Designed for "the bond man" — vocational training
  • Same curriculum served both practitioners and professors
  • Reveals no gap between academic and practitioner knowledge in 1917

Railroads Dominated

Railroad securities received far more detail than any other sector — reflecting their dominance as the main corporate securities class of the era.

Significance

  1. Snapshot of finance at its pre-theoretical moment: Before Fisher's influence, before Graham & Dodd, before Markowitz, before M&M — this is what "knowing finance" meant
  2. The present-value revolution had not occurred: Bond yield math existed but was not generalized to equity or business valuation
  3. The academic-practitioner boundary was nonexistent: Same curriculum, same books, same approach for both
  4. Finance was craft, not science: Institutional knowledge, legal knowledge, ratio analysis — not mathematical models
  5. The "bond man" paradigm: Investment meant bonds; stocks were partly speculative by definition

Paradigm

Pre-Split

Before the 1958 revolution — everyone used the same framework.

Era

The Consensus Era (1720–1957)

For over 200 years, practitioners and scholars alike valued assets by capitalizing earnings at market-observed yields.

Connections

  • The curriculum used: Chamberlain (1911), Lyon (1916), Gerstenberg (1915), Mead (1910) — all books we now have
  • What was missing: Fisher (1907) The Rate of Interest — published 10 years earlier but had NO visible influence on this curriculum
  • Would be transformed by: Graham & Dodd (1934), Williams (1938), Markowitz (1952), Modigliani & Miller (1958)
  • Compare with: Bonbright (1937) — who 20 years later explicitly applied capitalization-of-earnings to all property valuation
