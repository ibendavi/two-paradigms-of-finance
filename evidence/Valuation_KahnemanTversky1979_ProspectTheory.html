Key Finding

Kahneman & Tversky's 1979 Econometrica paper demolishes expected utility theory as a descriptive model of decision-making under risk and replaces it with prospect theory. Three systematic violations of expected utility: (1) the certainty effect â€” people overweight outcomes that are certain relative to merely probable; (2) the reflection effect â€” risk preferences reverse between gains and losses (risk-averse for gains, risk-seeking for losses); (3) the isolation effect â€” people discard shared components when choosing, leading to inconsistent preferences depending on framing. Prospect theory replaces utility-of-wealth with a value function defined on gains and losses relative to a reference point â€” concave for gains, convex for losses, and steeper for losses than gains (loss aversion). Decision weights Ï€(p) replace probabilities â€” overweighting small probabilities and underweighting large ones, with subcertainty: Ï€(p) + Ï€(1-p) < 1. This paper, with >80,000 citations, is arguably the most influential social science paper of the 20th century and launched behavioral economics.

Details

The Critique of Expected Utility (pp. 263-270)

Three systematic violations demonstrated through choice experiments:

Certainty Effect (Problems 1-4):

  • Problem 1: (4000, .80) vs. (3000, 1.00) â†’ 80% choose the certain 3000
  • Problem 2: (4000, .20) vs. (3000, .25) â†’ 65% choose the higher-EV gamble
  • This violates the independence axiom: multiplying both probabilities by .25 reverses preference
  • People overweight certainty: the reduction from 1.00 to .25 matters more than from .80 to .20

Reflection Effect (Problems 3'-8'):

  • Preferences reverse systematically between positive and negative domains
  • Risk averse for gains: prefer (3000) over (4000, .80)
  • Risk seeking for losses: prefer (-4000, .80) over (-3000)
  • "The reflection of prospects around 0 reverses the preference order" (p. 268)

Isolation Effect (Problems 10-12):

  • People simplify by ignoring shared components â†’ framing determines choice
  • Same final outcome framed differently produces different choices
  • Problem 11 vs. Problem 12: identical in terms of final states, but different frames â†’ different preferences

The Value Function (pp. 277-280)

Three properties:

  1. Defined on deviations from reference point â€” gains and losses, NOT final wealth
  2. Concave for gains, convex for losses â€” diminishing sensitivity in both directions
  3. Steeper for losses than gains â€” loss aversion; losses loom larger than gains

"The value function is (i) defined on deviations from the reference point; (ii) generally concave for gains and commonly convex for losses; (iii) steeper for losses than for gains." (p. 279)

The iconic S-shaped curve (Figure 3): an asymmetric S centered at the reference point. Loss aversion ratio approximately 2:1 (losing $100 feels roughly twice as bad as gaining $100 feels good).

Markowitz priority: "Markowitz [29] was the first to propose that utility be defined on gains and losses rather than on final asset positions" (p. 277). But Markowitz did not develop this into a full theory.

Applications and Implications (pp. 284-288)

  • Insurance: People overpay for certainty (prefer eliminating small risk to large reduction in larger risk)
  • Probabilistic insurance: Sharply undervalued relative to standard insurance â€” the residual uncertainty is overweighted
  • The Allais paradox: Naturally explained by the weighting function
  • Framing effects: Identical problems produce different choices depending on how outcomes are framed relative to the reference point

Significance

1. The Reference Point Revolution

Expected utility theory assumes people evaluate final wealth states. Prospect theory shows people evaluate changes â€” gains and losses relative to a reference point. This single insight transformed economics, explaining phenomena from the endowment effect to stock market behavior to labor supply decisions.

2. Loss Aversion

The asymmetry of the value function (losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains feel good) became one of the most robust findings in behavioral science. It explains the disposition effect (selling winners too early, holding losers too long), the equity premium puzzle (Benartzi & Thaler 1995), labor supply patterns, and consumer behavior.

3. The Bridge to Behavioral Finance

Though the paper itself contains no finance, it provided the theoretical foundation for behavioral finance:

  • De Bondt & Thaler (1985) overreaction â†’ prospect theory's loss aversion
  • Shiller (1981) excess volatility â†’ non-rational decision weights
  • Benartzi & Thaler (1995) equity premium puzzle â†’ myopic loss aversion
  • Odean (1998) disposition effect â†’ reference-dependent preferences

4. The Challenge to Rational Agent Models

Prospect theory showed that violations of rationality are not random errors but systematic, predictable patterns. This challenged the foundation of models like CAPM and EMH that assume rational agents. The question became: do these systematic biases survive in competitive markets?

5. Two Nobel Prizes

Kahneman received the Nobel Prize in Economics (2002) largely for this work. (Tversky had died in 1996 and was ineligible.) The paper also influenced Richard Thaler's Nobel (2017) for behavioral economics.
