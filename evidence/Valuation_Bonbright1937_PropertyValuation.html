Bonbright (1937): The Valuation of Property, Vol. 1

Bonbright, James C. *The Valuation of Property.* Vol. 1. New York: McGraw-Hill, 1937.

Key Finding

Bonbright's 1937 treatise is the most comprehensive pre-WWII treatment of capitalized-earnings valuation (V=E/r), applied across all legal and economic contexts. It provides explicit present value reasoning, dual capitalization rates for goodwill, and documents the historical evolution of stock valuation from balance-sheet emphasis to earnings capitalization to speculative earnings projection (the 1929 bubble). It directly engages with Graham & Dodd (1934) and Irving Fisher.

Details

Four Concepts of Value (p. 38)

  1. Value to the Owner — advantage conferred by ownership to a specific person
  2. Market Value — price obtainable in a sale
  3. Intrinsic Value — "not justified by those prospects of future yield that are assumed to govern the actions of investors" (p. 25); cites Graham & Dodd (1934) as best discussion
  4. Normal Market Value — hypothetical average/trend price

Five Valuation Methods

  1. Actual sale prices
  2. Historical cost / original cost
  3. Replacement cost / reproduction cost
  4. Depreciation from replacement cost new
  5. Capitalized income / capitalized earnings — "the value of an enterprise is dependent entirely on prospective earnings" (p. 237)
  6. Stock-and-bond method (sum of market values of securities)

Key Valuation Formulas

Basic capitalization: Earnings of $100,000/year at 5% = $2,000,000 enterprise value

Preferred stock perpetuity: $7 dividend / required return = $100/share (14.3x)

Dual-rate for goodwill:

  • Tangible assets: $1,000,000 (at replacement cost)
  • Normal earnings ($70,000) at 7% on tangibles
  • Excess earnings ($30,000) capitalized at 15% = $200,000 goodwill
  • Enterprise value = $1,200,000

Tax-adjusted capitalization:

  • Capitalize pre-tax income at (cap rate + tax rate): 5% + 2% = 7%

Hoskold formula for wasting assets:

  • Dual-rate with higher investment rate and lower sinking fund redemption rate

Evolution of Stock Valuation (p. 252, citing Graham & Dodd)

  1. First stage: Balance sheet / book value emphasis
  2. Second stage: Reported earnings — "10x earnings" rule became standard
  3. Third stage (1929): Projected future earnings capitalized at 20x-50x multiples

Capitalization Rate Problem

Bonbright identifies the choice of capitalization rate as "the central unresolved question" — rates "have been adopted by an admixture of tradition (such as that in favor of a 6 per cent rate or a 10 per cent rate) and guesswork" (p. 266). Enterprise valuation is "little more than guesswork" (p. 261).

Significance

  1. Most systematic pre-WWII valuation treatise: Covers every method, every legal context, with theoretical rigor
  2. Explicit V=E/r reasoning: Not just mentioned but worked through with numerical examples, risk adjustments, dual rates
  3. Bridge between law, economics, and finance: Synthesizes Fisher's capital theory with appraisal practice and legal standards
  4. Documents the 1929 valuation evolution: The three-stage progression from book value to earnings to speculative projection
  5. Identifies the discount rate as the key unsolved problem: The question that would dominate finance theory (CAPM, WACC, Fama-French) for the next 80 years

Paradigm

Pre-Split

Before the 1958 revolution — everyone used the same framework.

Era

The Consensus Era (1720–1957)

For over 200 years, practitioners and scholars alike valued assets by capitalizing earnings at market-observed yields.

Connections

  • Builds on: Fisher (1906/1930) The Rate of Interest / The Theory of Interest — capital-value theory
  • Engages with: Graham & Dodd (1934) Security Analysis — cites extensively on intrinsic value
  • Contemporary with: Williams (1938) Theory of Investment Value — both working on capitalization of income
  • Predates: Modigliani & Miller (1958) — cost of capital theory
  • Related to: Hoskold (1877) Engineer's Valuing Assistant — the earlier mining-property valuation framework
  • Same tradition as: Hutcheson (1720) in distinguishing "intrinsick value" from market price, but now formalized
