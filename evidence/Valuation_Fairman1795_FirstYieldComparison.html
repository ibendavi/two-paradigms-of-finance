Fairman (1795): Earliest Known Systematic Yield Comparison of Securities

Fairman, William. *The Stocks Examined and Compared: or, A Guide to Purchasers in the Public Funds.* London, 1795.

Key Finding

William Fairman's The Stocks Examined and Compared (1795) is the earliest known work providing systematic yield comparisons across all London-listed securities, with detailed tables enabling investors to compare value across different types of government funds, annuities, and securities. It introduces an early concept of internal rate of return (IRR) for terminable annuities.

Details

Purpose

A practical investor's guide â€” Fairman writes for "persons who have money to invest" and want to know which security offers the best return. This is explicitly a security comparison and selection tool.

The Five Major Tables

Table I: Comparative Yields of Government Funds

  • Lists every government security traded on the London market
  • For each, shows: face value, coupon rate, current market price, actual yield at market price
  • Allows side-by-side comparison of "3 per Cents" vs "4 per Cents" vs "5 per Cents" at various market prices
  • Key insight: A "3 per cent" fund at the right price can yield more than a "5 per cent" fund

Table II: Equivalent Prices Across Securities

  • Given a target yield rate, shows what price each security would need to trade at
  • Enables direct comparison: "If you're getting X% on 3% Consols, what's the equivalent price for 4% Consols?"
  • This is an early version of the concept of relative value / fair value across securities

Table III: Terminable Annuities â€” Present Value Tables

  • For annuities with fixed expiry dates (e.g., "Long Annuities" expiring 1860)
  • Computes present value at various discount rates
  • Shows the "real value" of what remains vs. the market price
  • Implicitly introduces the IRR concept: the rate at which PV of remaining payments = market price

Table IV: Compound Interest Tables

  • Standard compound interest and annuity tables
  • Powers of (1+r) for r = 3%, 3.5%, 4%, 4.5%, 5%
  • Present value of Â£1 annuity tables
  • These are the building blocks for all the other calculations

Table V: Life Annuity Valuation

  • Links to mortality tables (Northampton tables)
  • Values life-contingent annuities
  • Connects to early actuarial science (Price, Halley)

Conceptual Innovations

1. Yield as the common comparator

  • All securities are reduced to their yield at current market price
  • This allows apples-to-apples comparison across structurally different instruments
  • A conceptual ancestor of the modern yield curve / fixed income analytics

2. Internal Rate of Return (implicit)

  • For terminable annuities, Fairman solves for the rate that equates market price with PV of remaining payments
  • This IS the IRR concept, applied 150+ years before the term was coined

3. Information Asymmetry Discussion

  • Fairman notes that "gentlemen of the Stock Exchange" have an informational advantage over ordinary investors
  • Argues his tables level the playing field by giving the public the same analytical tools
  • Early discussion of informed vs. uninformed traders

4. Market Price vs. "Real Value"

  • Repeatedly distinguishes between what securities trade for vs. what they're worth based on cash flows
  • Same concept as Hutcheson's "intrinsick value" but more formally structured

Limitations

  • Covers only government securities and annuities, not corporate equity
  • Yield comparison is the dominant framework â€” no earnings analysis, no growth, no risk premium
  • Discount rates are not risk-adjusted

Significance

  1. Earliest known systematic security comparison tool: A precursor to modern fixed-income analytics, in 1795 pamphlet form. Enables any investor to compare all London-listed securities on a common basis.
  2. Bridge between bond valuation and equity valuation: While Fairman covers only government funds, the methodology (reduce everything to yield, compare) would eventually be extended to equity (earnings yield, dividend yield).
  3. Practitioner stream: Written explicitly for retail investors. This is "Wall Street" thinking (or "Change Alley" thinking) â€” practical tools for making money.
  4. Democratization of financial analysis: Fairman's explicit goal is to give ordinary investors the same tools professionals use â€” an early argument for transparency and investor protection.
  5. The yield-based paradigm: This work embodies the dominant pre-1930 approach to security valuation â€” everything is compared via yield. The shift to earnings-based and growth-based valuation would come later (Fisher 1906, Wiese 1930, Williams 1938).
