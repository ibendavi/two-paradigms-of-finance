Ross (1976): The Arbitrage Theory of Capital Asset Pricing ‚Äî Beyond CAPM

Ross, Stephen A. "The Arbitrage Theory of Capital Asset Pricing." *Journal of Economic Theory* 13, no. 3 (December 1976): 341-360.

Key Finding

Ross's 1976 Journal of Economic Theory paper develops the Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT) as an alternative to the CAPM. The APT is both more general than CAPM (weaker assumptions) and less specific (doesn't identify which factors matter).

What APT Does NOT Specify

  • Which factors matter ‚Äî the theory says expected returns depend linearly on factor loadings but does not identify the factors
  • How many factors ‚Äî could be 1, 3, 5, or more
  • The risk premiums ‚Äî Œ≥_j must be estimated empirically
  • Whether the market portfolio is a factor ‚Äî it might be, but it plays no special role

CAPM as a Special Case (p. 354)

If returns have a single-factor structure and that factor is the market portfolio, APT reduces to CAPM. But APT allows for multiple factors and does not require that any of them be the market.

Significance

1. Arbitrage Replaces Optimization

The CAPM derives pricing from investor optimization (mean-variance portfolio choice). The APT derives pricing from no-arbitrage alone ‚Äî a much weaker and more powerful assumption. This methodological shift (from optimization to no-arbitrage) would also underpin Black-Scholes (1973) and the entire derivatives pricing framework. M&M (1958) pioneered the no-arbitrage approach; Ross generalized it to asset pricing.

2. Multiple Factors Allowed

The CAPM says only market beta matters. The APT says multiple factors can matter. This opened the door to multi-factor models, culminating in Fama-French (1993) three-factor model and subsequent extensions. APT provided the theoretical justification for using size, value, momentum, and other factors.

3. The Market Portfolio Problem Solved (Partially)

Roll (1977) showed the CAPM is untestable because the true market portfolio is unobservable. The APT sidesteps this ‚Äî the market portfolio plays no special role, so its observability doesn't matter. However, APT introduces its own identification problem: which factors to use.

4. Theory Outruns Empirics

APT is theoretically elegant but empirically underdetermined. It says factors matter but not which ones. The subsequent empirical literature (Chen, Roll & Ross 1986; Fama & French 1993) attempted to identify the relevant factors, but the choice remains contested.
