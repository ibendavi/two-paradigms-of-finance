Ohlson (1995): Earnings, Book Values, and Dividends in Equity Valuation

Ohlson, James A. "Earnings, Book Values, and Dividends in Equity Valuation." *Contemporary Accounting Research* 11, no. 2 (Spring 1995): 661-687. (Ohlson was at Columbia University.)

Key Finding

Ohlson's 1995 Contemporary Accounting Research paper provides the formal theoretical model linking accounting data (earnings, book values, dividends) to equity valuation. The model rests on three assumptions: (A1) the present value of expected dividends determines price (PVED), (A2) the clean surplus relation holds (book value changes = earnings minus dividends), and (A3) abnormal earnings follow a linear autoregressive process. The resulting valuation function is P_t = y_t + Œ±‚ÇÅx_t^a + Œ±‚ÇÇv_t ‚Äî price equals book value plus a linear function of abnormal earnings and "other information." Two special cases are illuminating: when abnormal earnings are perfectly persistent (œâ = 1), the model yields P_t = œÜx_t - d_t, a pure earnings capitalization model where œÜ = R_f/(R_f - 1) is an earnings multiplier (approximately P/E = 1/r); when abnormal earnings are entirely transitory (œâ = 0), the model yields P_t = y_t, a pure book value model. The general case is a weighted average of capitalized earnings and book value. For the EPS project, this paper formalizes the link between earnings capitalization and equity pricing ‚Äî the œÜ = R_f/(R_f - 1) earnings multiplier is exactly the P/E ratio that the EPS paradigm treats as the fundamental pricing mechanism, and Ohlson's model shows this emerges naturally when abnormal earnings are persistent.

Significance

1. The Earnings Multiplier œÜ = R_f/(R_f - 1) IS the P/E Ratio

Ohlson's earnings multiplier œÜ = R_f/(R_f - 1) ‚âà 1/r is exactly the P/E ratio in the EPS paradigm's pricing formula P = EPS √ó P/E. When abnormal earnings are persistent (œâ close to 1), Ohlson's model reduces to P = œÜ √ó EPS ‚Äî earnings capitalization in its purest form. The EPS paradigm's claim that investors price stocks by capitalizing earnings is formally justified by Ohlson's model under the assumption of persistent earnings.

2. The Special Case œâ = 1 Is the EPS Paradigm's Baseline

The pure earnings capitalization case (P_t = œÜx_t - d_t when œâ = 1) is the theoretical benchmark for the EPS paradigm. If earnings are persistent ‚Äî and the evidence on earnings persistence (e.g., Ou & Penman 1989, Bernard & Thomas 1990) suggests they largely are ‚Äî then price is determined by capitalizing current earnings at the earnings yield. Book value is irrelevant. This is exactly what the EPS paradigm claims: P = EPS √ó P/E, where the P/E multiplier is approximately 1/r.

3. œâ Measures the "Earnings Quality" That Determines the P/E Multiple

In the EPS paradigm, the P/E multiple comes from "comps" ‚Äî comparable firms in the same industry and risk class. Ohlson's model reveals what drives this multiple theoretically: it is the persistence of abnormal earnings (œâ). High-œâ firms (persistent competitive advantage) deserve high P/E ratios; low-œâ firms (transitory earnings) deserve low P/E ratios. This maps onto the practitioner distinction between "quality" and "value" stocks.

4. Book Value Matters Only When Earnings Are Transitory

Ohlson shows that book value enters the valuation only when œâ < 1 ‚Äî when abnormal earnings are mean-reverting. For stable, mature firms with persistent earnings (the typical case for the EPS paradigm), book value is essentially irrelevant to pricing. This explains why practitioners focus on EPS and P/E rather than book value: for most firms, earnings persistence is high enough that capitalized earnings dominate the valuation.

5. Dividend Irrelevance Is Built Into the Model

Property (P3) ‚Äî ‚àÇP_t/‚àÇd_t = -1 ‚Äî means dividends don't create value in Ohlson's model. This is consistent with the EPS paradigm where dividends are a payout decision, not a value-creation mechanism. Managers focused on maximizing EPS would only pay dividends if doing so doesn't reduce future EPS below the level that would prevail under retention.

6. The Model Bridges Accounting and Asset Pricing

Ohlson's model is the theoretical bridge between the accounting literature (Ball & Brown 1968, earnings have information content) and the asset pricing literature (Basu 1977, E/P predicts returns). It shows formally that if accounting data follows certain dynamics, then earnings capitalization IS the rational pricing framework. The EPS paradigm's claim that P = EPS √ó P/E is not ad hoc ‚Äî it is the implication of Ohlson's model for firms with persistent earnings.
