Andrade (1997): Do Appearances Matter? The Impact of EPS Accretion and Dilution on Stock Prices

Andrade, Gregor. "Do Appearances Matter? The Impact of EPS Accretion and Dilution on Stock Prices." Working paper, Harvard Business School, 1997. (Later cited as Andrade 1999 in some references.)

Key Finding

Andrade's 1997 Harvard Business School working paper provides the first direct evidence that EPS accretion and dilution in mergers affect acquirer stock prices. Using 4,814 mergers from 1975-1994, Andrade computes two measures of EPS accretion â€” MKT_ACC (market accretion, what the deal would deliver at market prices without a premium) and ACQ_ACC (acquisition accretion, what the deal actually delivers at the terms paid) â€” and shows both significantly predict acquirer announcement returns and long-term abnormal performance. The announcement-return coefficient on accretion is approximately 0.3, implying a "three-to-one" relationship: a 1% change in the acquirer's earnings yield from the deal produces roughly a 0.3% return. Long-term abnormal returns over 18 months also increase with accretion, at roughly 0.1% per month (1.3% annualized), with the effect concentrated in dilutive deals and firms with low institutional ownership. Critically, EPS accretion is uncorrelated with changes in actual operating cash flows â€” the effect is purely cosmetic (accounting-driven), yet the market responds. Pooling accretion (which is entirely an accounting artifact â€” the target's book value is substituted for the purchase price) has the same magnitude and significance as purchase accretion, further confirming the cosmetic nature. The market response is roughly one-tenth of what a "naive practitioner view" (coefficient of 10) would predict â€” investors partially but not fully see through EPS accretion. For the EPS project, this is the earliest paper directly testing the M&A EPS accretion mechanism and finding that appearances DO matter: cosmetic EPS changes from mergers move stock prices, exactly as the EPS paradigm predicts.

Details

Section 1: Introduction (pp. 1-5)

The central question: Practitioners universally evaluate mergers based on whether the deal is EPS-accretive or EPS-dilutive to the acquirer. Does this cosmetic accounting metric actually affect stock prices?

The practitioner view: Investment bankers and corporate managers treat EPS accretion as a primary criterion for deal evaluation. A deal that is EPS-accretive is viewed favorably; an EPS-dilutive deal must be "justified" by future growth. The naive practitioner prediction: a 1% increase in the acquirer's earnings yield from the deal should produce a return equal to the inverse of the P/E ratio Ã— the change â€” roughly a coefficient of 10 on the accretion variable.

The academic view: In efficient markets, cosmetic EPS changes should not affect value. Only changes in real cash flows should matter. EPS accretion is a mechanical result of combining two earnings streams at different multiples â€” no real value is created.

Two accretion measures:

  • MKT_ACC (market accretion): The hypothetical EPS accretion if the acquirer could buy the target at market prices (no premium). Computed as: [(E_T/P_T) - (E_A/P_A)] Ã— relative size. This measures the raw earnings yield differential between target and acquirer.
  • ACQ_ACC (acquisition accretion): The actual EPS accretion at the deal's terms (including premium paid). Computed using the actual exchange ratio or deal value. This is what the acquirer actually delivers to shareholders.

Section 2: Data and Methodology (pp. 5-12)

Sample: 4,814 mergers from SDC, 1975-1994. Includes stock deals, cash deals, and mixed payment. For EPS accretion to be computable, both acquirer and target must have earnings data.

Key variable construction:

  • Earnings yield = (Net income before extraordinary items + interest expense Ã— (1-tax rate)) / market cap
  • MKT_ACC = (target earnings yield - acquirer earnings yield) Ã— (target market cap / acquirer market cap)
  • ACQ_ACC = same formula but using actual deal value instead of target market cap

Summary statistics: Mean MKT_ACC = 0.82% (median 0.36%); mean ACQ_ACC = -0.06% (median -0.28%). The premium converts most accretive deals (at market prices) into dilutive deals (at acquisition terms). But substantial variation exists in both directions.

The "three-to-one" relationship: A coefficient of 0.3 means that for every 1 percentage point increase in the acquirer's earnings yield from the deal, the announcement return increases by approximately 0.3%. If the naive practitioner view were correct (market fully capitalizes EPS accretion), the coefficient would be approximately 10 (the P/E ratio). The actual coefficient of 0.3 is roughly one-tenth of this.

Interpretation: The market does respond to EPS accretion, but partially â€” it does not fully capitalize the cosmetic EPS change. Roughly 3% of the EPS accretion "leaks through" into stock prices.

Controls: Results hold after controlling for deal size, payment method, industry relatedness, hostile vs. friendly, and year fixed effects.

Section 4: Long-Term Abnormal Returns (pp. 20-26)

Calendar-time portfolio regressions (Fama-French approach):

Portfolios sorted by ACQ_ACC (accretive vs. dilutive deals) are held for 18 months post-announcement. The coefficient on accretion in monthly return regressions is approximately 0.1% per month, or roughly 1.3% annualized.

Key findings:

  • The positive relation between accretion and long-term returns is concentrated in the first 18 months after the deal
  • The effect is stronger for dilutive deals â€” the market penalizes dilutive deals more than it rewards accretive ones
  • The effect is stronger for firms with low institutional ownership â€” consistent with less sophisticated investors driving the EPS accretion response
  • The effect is economically meaningful but far smaller than the naive practitioner prediction

Section 5: Pooling vs. Purchase Accounting (pp. 26-30)

This section is critical for the EPS paradigm.

Under pooling accounting (pre-2001), the acquirer records the target's assets at book value. Under purchase accounting, assets are recorded at fair (purchase) value and any excess is goodwill, which was then amortized against earnings.

Pooling accretion is PURELY cosmetic: It arises solely because the target's book value (used in pooling) differs from its market value. There is no economic difference between a pooling and purchase deal â€” the same assets, same cash flows, same business combination. The only difference is the accounting treatment of the target's book value and goodwill.

Result: Pooling accretion has the SAME magnitude and significance as purchase accretion in predicting announcement returns and long-term performance. The market responds equally to a purely accounting-driven EPS change as to one that reflects actual earnings yield differentials.

This is powerful evidence that appearances DO matter: an EPS change that is entirely an accounting artifact moves stock prices.

Section 6: Operating Performance (pp. 30-33)

The acid test: If EPS accretion reflected real economic value creation, it should predict changes in operating cash flows.

Result: EPS accretion is UNCORRELATED with changes in operating performance (cash flow from operations, EBITDA). Accretive deals do NOT produce better operating outcomes than dilutive deals.

This confirms that EPS accretion is cosmetic â€” it does not reflect real value creation. Yet the market responds to it (Sections 3-4). This combination â€” cosmetic metric, real price impact â€” is exactly what the EPS paradigm predicts.

Section 7: Summary (pp. 33-35)

Andrade concludes that "appearances matter" â€” EPS accretion affects stock prices even though it provides no information about real economic value. The effect is partial: roughly one-tenth of what a naive view would predict, suggesting the market partially but not fully sees through cosmetic EPS changes.

Significance

1. First Direct Test of EPS Accretion in M&A

This is the earliest paper to directly test whether the practitioner obsession with EPS accretion/dilution in mergers has a real stock price effect. It confirms that the EPS accretion criterion â€” the same criterion investment bankers use to evaluate every deal â€” is reflected in market prices. This is foundational evidence for the EPS paradigm's M&A predictions.

2. Appearances DO Matter â€” Cosmetic EPS Moves Prices

The combination of three findings â€” (a) EPS accretion predicts announcement returns, (b) EPS accretion is uncorrelated with operating performance, and (c) pooling accretion (purely accounting-driven) has the same effect as purchase accretion â€” proves that cosmetic EPS changes move stock prices. In the NPV paradigm, this should not happen. In the EPS paradigm, it is expected: P = EPS Ã— P/E, so anything that changes reported EPS changes price.

3. The Market Partially Capitalizes EPS Accretion

The coefficient of 0.3 (vs. ~10 for full capitalization) means the market is somewhere between fully seeing through EPS accretion (coefficient = 0) and fully capitalizing it (coefficient â‰ˆ 10). This partial response is consistent with the EPS paradigm: the market uses EPS in pricing but is not entirely naive â€” some investors recognize the cosmetic nature. The "truth" lies between the NPV paradigm's prediction (zero response) and the naive EPS paradigm's prediction (full capitalization).

4. Low Institutional Ownership Amplifies the Effect

The finding that low institutional ownership firms show stronger EPS accretion effects suggests that less sophisticated investors are more responsive to EPS-based metrics. This connects to the EPS paradigm's claim that earnings capitalization is the default pricing mechanism â€” more prevalent among retail and less analytically sophisticated investors.

5. Pooling Accretion Confirms the Accounting Channel

Pooling accretion is a perfect test case: it is 100% accounting artifact, zero economic substance. The fact that the market responds to pooling accretion identically to purchase accretion is perhaps the single strongest piece of evidence that reported EPS â€” not economic value â€” drives pricing. This directly supports P = EPS Ã— P/E.

6. The One-Tenth Ratio Matches Hribar et al.'s Partial Discounting

Andrade's finding that the market response is ~1/10 of the naive prediction parallels Hribar, Jenkins & Johnson's (2006) finding that the market partially discounts repurchase-induced EPS. Both papers find the same pattern: the market responds to cosmetic EPS changes but at a fraction of the full-capitalization rate. The EPS paradigm accommodates this: P = EPS Ã— P/E, but the implicit P/E applied to cosmetic EPS may be lower than the P/E applied to organic EPS.

7. Dilutive Deals Are Penalized More Than Accretive Deals Are Rewarded

The asymmetry â€” stronger effects for dilutive deals â€” is consistent with the practitioner world's asymmetric treatment of accretion and dilution. An EPS-dilutive deal requires extensive justification ("it will be accretive in year 3"); an accretive deal is presumed good. This asymmetric hurdle is exactly what the EPS paradigm describes.
