Williams (1938): The Theory of Investment Value ‚Äî The Dividend Discount Model

Williams, John Burr. *The Theory of Investment Value.* Amsterdam: North-Holland, 1938. (Harvard doctoral dissertation; supervised by Schumpeter, Leontief.)

Key Finding

Williams' 1938 Harvard dissertation formalizes the dividend discount model (DDM): V = sum of all future dividends discounted to present value. He argues "a stock is worth only what you can get out of it" ‚Äî dividends, not earnings, are the correct basis. He also states the Law of Conservation of Investment Value (capital structure irrelevance for total firm value), anticipating Modigliani-Miller (1958) by 20 years.

Dividends, Not Earnings (pp. 57-58)

"Earnings are only a means to an end, and the means should not be mistaken for the end. Therefore we must say that a stock derives its value from its dividends, not its earnings. In short, a stock is worth only what you can get out of it."

"A cow for her milk, / A hen for her eggs, / And a stock, by heck, / For her dividends."

But reconciles with "earnings make prices": retained earnings only matter insofar as they generate future dividends. The DDM captures reinvested earnings through the larger future dividends they produce.

Investment Value vs. Market Price (Ch. I, p. 3)

  • Investment value = present worth of all future dividends (objective, calculable)
  • Market price = set by marginal opinion (may be wrong)

"Separate and distinct things not to be confused are real worth and market price."

Buying below investment value ‚Üí can never lose (hold for income). Buying above ‚Üí only hope is finding a greater fool.

Law of Conservation of Investment Value (pp. 72-73)

"If the investment value of an enterprise as a whole is the present worth of all its future distributions to security holders, then this value in no wise depends on what the company's capitalization is."

This IS Modigliani-Miller Proposition I, stated 20 years earlier. Williams compares it to the conservation of matter or energy.

The Discount Rate

  • Each investor should use their own personal rate (reflecting their opportunity cost)
  • For "investors in general," use the market pure interest rate
  • Allows time-varying discount rates ‚Äî different rate for each year (highly unusual for 1938)
  • Risk premium: x = i - √Æ (yield minus riskless rate). "Strictly speaking, there is no risk in buying if the price is right" given adequate diversification.

Growth Stocks (Ch. VII)

Williams presents formulas for stocks with expected growth in dividends ‚Äî different from the later Gordon Growth Model but covering the same territory. The growth rate g enters the formula.

Notable Predecessors Cited

  • Wiese (1930) ‚Äî "Investing for True Values," Barron's ‚Äî cited as pioneer of DDM for stocks
  • Preinreich (1935) ‚Äî The Nature of Dividends ‚Äî cited for related work
  • Fisher (1907/1930) ‚Äî present value framework

Case Studies (Book II)

Applied the DDM to real companies: General Motors, U.S. Steel, Phoenix Insurance, AT&T, Consolidated Gas, American & Foreign Power. This made the book both theoretical and practical.

Significance

  1. First formal DDM for stocks: The algebraic framework that all subsequent equity valuation builds on
  2. Dividends over earnings: Settled (temporarily) the debate over what drives stock value
  3. M&M anticipation: Conservation of Investment Value = capital structure irrelevance, 20 years early
  4. Time-varying discount rates: Unusual sophistication for 1938
  5. Bridges theory and practice: Case studies applying the model to real companies
  6. Academic stream: Harvard dissertation, but written to be practically applicable
