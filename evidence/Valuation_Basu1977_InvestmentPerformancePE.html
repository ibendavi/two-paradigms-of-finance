Basu (1977): Investment Performance of Common Stocks in Relation to Their Price-Earnings Ratios: A Test of the Efficient Market Hypothesis

Basu, Sanjoy. "Investment Performance of Common Stocks in Relation to Their Price-Earnings Ratios: A Test of the Efficient Market Hypothesis." *Journal of Finance* 32, no. 3 (June 1977): 663-682. (Basu was at McMaster University, Hamilton, Ontario.)

Key Finding

Basu's 1977 Journal of Finance paper provides the seminal empirical evidence of the "P/E effect": low P/E stocks systematically outperform high P/E stocks on a risk-adjusted basis. Using 753 NYSE industrial firms over April 1957-March 1971, Basu forms five portfolios annually ranked by P/E ratio and finds that the lowest P/E quintile (E) earned 13.55% annually vs. 9.34% for the highest P/E quintile (A), with Jensen's alpha of +4.7% (t=3.98) for low P/E and -3.3% (t=-2.62) for high P/E — despite nearly identical systematic risk (betas of 0.99 and 1.11 respectively). The results survive controls for taxes, differential beta risk, transaction costs, and multiple investor types. Basu concludes that P/E ratio information is "not fully reflected in security prices," constituting a violation of semi-strong form market efficiency. For the EPS project, this paper is foundational: the P/E effect is exactly what the earnings capitalization framework predicts — the earnings yield (E/P) IS the expected return, so high E/P firms should earn higher returns.

Details

Data and Methodology (pp. 663-666)

Sample: All industrial firms listed on the NYSE during the 14-year period April 1957 to March 1971, with fiscal year ending December 31 and continuous price and earnings data. This yields approximately 500-750 firms per year (753 firms total across the period).

Portfolio construction: At April 1 of each year t, firms are ranked by their P/E ratio as of December 31 of year t-1 (using fiscal year earnings). Five portfolios (A through E) are formed:

  • Portfolio A: highest P/E quintile (median P/E = 35.8)
  • Portfolio B: second highest (median P/E = 19.1)
  • Portfolio C: middle (median P/E = 15.2)
  • Portfolio D: fourth (median P/E = 12.8)
  • Portfolio E: lowest P/E quintile (median P/E = 9.8)

Firms with zero or negative earnings are excluded (a methodological choice Basu acknowledges may bias results).

Return measurement: Monthly returns (dividends + capital appreciation) computed for the 12-month holding period April 1 to March 31. The 3-month lag between fiscal year end and portfolio formation ensures all P/E information is publicly available at portfolio formation date.

Benchmark: Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). Jensen's differential return (alpha) measures risk-adjusted performance:

δ_p = R̄_p - [R̄_f + β̂_p(R̄_m - R̄_f)]

Main Results: The P/E Effect (Table 1, p. 667)

Portfolio Median P/E Avg Annual Return Systematic Risk (β) Jensen's δ t-stat
A (high P/E) 35.8 9.34% 1.11 -0.033 -2.62
B 19.1 9.28% 1.05 -0.031 -3.24
C 15.2 11.67% 1.04 +0.005 0.56
D 12.8 13.55% 1.01 +0.022 2.49
E (low P/E) 9.8 13.55% 0.99 +0.047 3.98

Key observations:

  1. Returns increase monotonically from high to low P/E (with D and E tied at 13.55%).
  2. Betas are nearly IDENTICAL across portfolios (range: 0.99 to 1.11) — the return differential is NOT explained by systematic risk.
  3. Jensen's alpha is economically and statistically significant at both extremes: -3.3% for high P/E, +4.7% for low P/E.
  4. The spread between A and E is 4.21% in raw returns and 8.0% in risk-adjusted returns.

After-Tax Results (Table 2, p. 669)

Using Brennan's (1970) after-tax CAPM, results are qualitatively identical. Portfolio E's alpha is +3.9% (t=3.31), Portfolio A's alpha is -3.6% (t=-2.87). Taxes cannot explain the P/E effect.

Sub-Portfolios by Beta Within P/E Class (Table 3, p. 671)

Within each P/E quintile, Basu forms two sub-portfolios by beta (high vs. low systematic risk). Key finding: Jensen's delta "tends to be somewhat larger" for the low P/E class across most beta levels, confirming that the P/E effect is not a disguised beta effect. The P/E information adds value AFTER controlling for beta.

Random Portfolio Test (Table 4, p. 674)

For each P/E portfolio, 20 random portfolios of equal size are drawn from the full sample (without P/E sorting). These random portfolios have equivalent beta to the P/E-sorted portfolios. Results:

  • Portfolio E (low P/E) outperforms its random counterparts at the 0.05 level
  • Portfolio A (high P/E) underperforms its random counterparts at the 0.05 level
  • Middle portfolios (B, C, D) show no significant difference from random

This confirms the P/E effect is not an artifact of portfolio construction methodology.

Wealth Relatives (Table 5, pp. 676-677)

"Wealth relatives" compare terminal wealth from P/E portfolios to terminal wealth from random portfolios at each decile of the wealth distribution. At every decile:

  • Low P/E (E) dominates its random benchmark
  • High P/E (A) is dominated by its random benchmark

The dominance is consistent across the entire distribution, not driven by outliers.

Transaction Costs and Four Investor Classes (Table 6, p. 679)

Basu considers four types of investors:

  1. Tax-exempt reallocator (reallocates across P/E quintiles annually): excess return +3.51% after costs
  2. Tax-paying reallocator: excess return +1.94% after costs
  3. Tax-exempt trader (switches between low P/E and T-bills based on market timing): excess return +1.08% after costs
  4. Tax-paying trader: excess return +0.40% after costs

The portfolio reallocators (types 1-2) earn economically significant excess returns even after transaction costs. The traders (types 3-4) earn smaller but still positive excess returns. Transaction costs reduce but do not eliminate the P/E effect.

Interpretation: Market Disequilibria (pp. 680-681)

Basu's conclusion is carefully stated:

"the P/E ratio information was not 'fully reflected' in security prices in as rapid a manner as postulated by the semi-strong form of the efficient market hypothesis."

He notes three possible interpretations: (1) the CAPM is misspecified (omitted risk factors), (2) the P/E ratio proxies for a true risk factor not captured by beta, or (3) the market is genuinely inefficient with respect to P/E information. Basu favors interpretation (3) but acknowledges he cannot definitively rule out (1) or (2).

Significance

1. The P/E Effect IS the Earnings Yield Effect

In the EPS paradigm, the earnings yield (E/P) is the cost of equity — the expected return on a stock. Basu's finding that high E/P (low P/E) stocks earn higher returns is not an "anomaly" requiring explanation; it is the DEFINITION of earnings-based pricing. If investors price stocks as P = EPS × P/E, then E/P = EPS/P is the yield they earn. High yield means high return. This is exactly what Basu documents.

2. Beta Does Not Explain Cross-Sectional Returns — But E/P Does

Basu's most striking finding is that betas are nearly identical across P/E quintiles (0.99 to 1.11), yet returns differ enormously (9.3% to 13.6%). This means the CAPM's risk measure (beta) fails to explain the return variation that the earnings yield explains perfectly. The earnings yield is a better measure of expected returns than beta — exactly as the EPS paradigm claims.

3. The Spread Is the Earnings Yield Spread

The 4.2% raw return spread between high and low P/E portfolios (13.55% - 9.34%) corresponds closely to the difference in earnings yields. Portfolio E has a median P/E of 9.8 (E/P ≈ 10.2%), while Portfolio A has a median P/E of 35.8 (E/P ≈ 2.8%). The 7.4% E/P spread maps onto the 4.2% return spread, with the compression reflecting growth (high P/E firms have some growth embedded) and mean reversion (the Molodovsky effect documented by Ou & Penman 1989).

4. Not a "Behavioral Bias" — A Pricing Framework

Basu interprets his results as evidence of market "disequilibria" or inefficiency. In the EPS paradigm, there is no inefficiency: the market correctly prices stocks using earnings capitalization, and the earnings yield IS the expected return. The "puzzle" exists only if you assume the CAPM is the correct pricing model and beta is the correct risk measure.

5. Historical Priority: Pre-Dates Fama-French

Basu (1977) documents the value premium 15 years before Fama & French (1992) attribute it to HML. The P/E effect is the original "value effect," and it is explicitly an earnings-based effect — not a book-to-market effect. The EPS paradigm's emphasis on the earnings yield as the cost of equity aligns with Basu's original finding more naturally than the Fama-French factor framework.

6. Transaction Costs Cannot Eliminate the Effect

The survival of the P/E effect after transaction costs (Table 6) means it is not merely a theoretical curiosity. Portfolio reallocators earning 2-3.5% excess returns after costs suggests the earnings yield spread is a real, exploitable feature of asset prices — consistent with it being a fundamental pricing relationship rather than a statistical artifact.

Connections

  • Nicholson (1960, 1968) — Basu cites Nicholson's early work documenting the P/E effect in common stocks. Nicholson's 1960 FAJ article is the earliest systematic study.
  • Miller & Modigliani (1961) — MM's dividend irrelevance assumes markets are efficient. Basu's P/E effect challenges the efficiency assumption that underlies MM.
  • Ball & Brown (1968) — Ball & Brown's post-earnings announcement drift is a related earnings-based anomaly. Both show that earnings information is not fully incorporated into prices.
  • Fama (1970) — Basu explicitly tests Fama's semi-strong form efficient market hypothesis and rejects it.
  • Jensen (1968, 1969) — Basu uses Jensen's measure of portfolio performance (alpha) as the primary test statistic.
  • Black, Jensen & Scholes (1972) — The CAPM benchmark model. Basu's results show the CAPM fails to explain P/E-sorted returns.
  • Brennan (1970) — Provides the after-tax CAPM used in Table 2.
  • Basu (1983) — Basu's follow-up paper in JFE provides further evidence on the relationship between E/P, firm size, and returns, controlling for the size effect (Banz 1981).
  • Ou & Penman (1989) — Document the P/E effect and the Molodovsky effect (P/E mean-reversion due to transitory earnings). The P/E effect in Ou & Penman is the same phenomenon Basu documents.
  • Jaffe, Keim & Westerfield (1989) — Show E/P predicts returns after controlling for beta, size, and January effects — extending Basu's result.
  • Fama & French (1992) — "The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns" shows beta has no explanatory power for returns after controlling for size and book-to-market. Basu's P/E effect is subsumed by book-to-market in Fama-French, but the EPS paradigm argues this gets the causation backward: the earnings yield is the fundamental pricing variable.
  • Graham & Dodd (1934) — Graham & Dodd's value investing framework uses the earnings yield as the measure of cheapness. Basu's low P/E outperformance is the empirical validation of Graham & Dodd's approach.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS) — Basu's P/E effect is the foundational empirical fact for the EPS paradigm's claim that the earnings yield is the cost of equity. If E/P is the expected return, then high E/P stocks should earn higher returns — which is exactly what Basu (1977) documents.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (Backward-looking AP) — Basu shows that a backward-looking measure (last year's E/P) predicts forward-looking returns. This is the essence of backward-looking asset pricing: current earnings determine expected returns.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (Equity risk premium) — The earnings yield spread (E/P - rf) can be interpreted as the equity risk premium in the EPS paradigm. Basu's finding that E/P predicts returns supports the view that the ERP is simply the observable spread between the earnings yield and the risk-free rate.
