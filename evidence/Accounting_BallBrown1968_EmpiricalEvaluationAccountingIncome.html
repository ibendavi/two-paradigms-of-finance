Ball & Brown (1968): An Empirical Evaluation of Accounting Income Numbers

Ball, Ray, and Philip Brown. "An Empirical Evaluation of Accounting Income Numbers." *Journal of Accounting Research* 6, no. 2 (Autumn 1968): 159-178. (Ball was at the University of Chicago; Brown was at the University of Western Australia and the University of Chicago.)

Key Finding

Ball & Brown's 1968 Journal of Accounting Research paper is the foundational event study in accounting and finance, establishing that accounting income numbers have substantial information content for stock prices. Using 261 NYSE firms over fiscal years 1957-1965, they develop the Abnormal Performance Index (API) methodology and show that approximately 50% of all information about a firm available during a year is captured by that year's income number. However, 85-90% of this content is anticipated by the market before the annual report is released, with only 10-15% of the information value arriving in the announcement month itself. Post-announcement drift persists for approximately two months. For the EPS project, this paper provides the earliest systematic evidence that earnings are the CENTRAL information variable for equity pricing â€” prices move with earnings, earnings predict returns, and the market's primary task is forecasting earnings.

Details

The Research Question (pp. 159-160)

Ball & Brown pose a deceptively simple question: do accounting income numbers have information content? Prior to this paper, accounting theorists debated the "usefulness" of income measures on purely theoretical grounds. Ball & Brown propose an empirical test: if income numbers convey information, then observing the income number should be associated with stock price changes. If not, income numbers are mere noise.

"An empirical evaluation of accounting income numbers requires agreement as to what real-world outcome constitutes an appropriate test of usefulness. Because net income is a number of particular interest to investors, the outcome we use as a predictive criterion is the investment decision as it is reflected in security prices." (p. 159)

Figure 1: The Famous Drift Chart (pp. 167-168)

Figure 1 is one of the most reproduced figures in finance. It plots the API for three portfolios:

  2. Markets anticipate most of the information before the announcement

50% of all information: Ball & Brown estimate that about half of all the information about a firm that becomes available during a year is captured by the annual income number: "of all the information about an individual firm which becomes available during a year, one-half or more is captured in that year's income number" (p. 169).

85-90% anticipated: Of the information contained in the income number, 85-90% has been anticipated by the market before the annual report is released. Only 10-15% is genuinely "new news" at the announcement date.

Sources of Anticipation (pp. 170-174)

Ball & Brown identify four channels through which the market anticipates earnings:

  1. Interim reports â€” Quarterly earnings releases during the fiscal year
  2. Industry information â€” Economy-wide and industry-level earnings trends
  3. Non-accounting sources â€” Dividends, capital expenditure announcements, management forecasts
  4. Competing information providers â€” Analysts, credit agencies, financial press

The key insight: earnings are anticipated because many information sources provide overlapping signals about the firm's profitability. The annual income report largely confirms what the market already knows.

Conclusion (pp. 175-176)

"The results are consistent with the view that the market anticipates most of the content of the annual report... The annual income report does not rate highly as a timely medium... its content is useful." (p. 176)

Ball & Brown note that while the annual report is not timely (most information is old by the time it arrives), it is still useful because the income number CONFIRMS or DISCONFIRMS the market's expectations. The 10-15% of information value that arrives at the announcement is economically meaningful.

Significance

1. Earnings Are the Central Information Variable

Ball & Brown's finding that 50% of all annual information about a firm is captured by its income number directly supports the EPS paradigm's view that earnings are the central variable for equity pricing. The market doesn't track cash flows, book values, or dividends with the same intensity â€” it tracks earnings. This is why P = EPS Ã— P/E is the pricing framework: earnings are the single most informative variable about firm value.

2. The Market FORECASTS Earnings â€” That's Its Job

The finding that 85-90% of income information is anticipated before the annual report confirms that the market's primary activity is forecasting earnings. Stock prices adjust continuously as new information about future earnings arrives. This is exactly the EPS paradigm's view: investors forecast next-twelve-month EPS and apply a P/E multiple. The market is an earnings-forecasting machine.

3. EPS and Net Income Are Informationally Equivalent

The 93% concordance between net income and EPS forecast errors (Table 6) and the nearly identical API patterns for all three income variables (Table 5) show that EPS â€” the variable managers and investors focus on â€” captures essentially the same information as net income. The EPS paradigm's focus on EPS rather than broader income measures is empirically justified.

4. Post-Announcement Drift â€” The Earnings Yield Connection

The post-announcement drift (prices continue adjusting for ~2 months after the report) will later become one of the most robust anomalies in finance. In the EPS paradigm, drift occurs because the market is adjusting the earnings yield: a positive earnings surprise raises E in P = EPS Ã— P/E, and the price adjustment may be gradual as investors update their forward EPS estimates. The drift is consistent with earnings-based pricing where earnings information is incorporated incrementally.

5. The API Methodology and Earnings Events

Ball & Brown invented the event study methodology that will be used by hundreds of subsequent papers studying earnings announcements, stock splits, mergers, and other corporate events. The methodology implicitly assumes that stock returns are driven by information events â€” and the single most informative event category is earnings. This methodological legacy reinforces the centrality of earnings to equity pricing.

6. Accounting Numbers ARE Informative â€” Not Garbled

Ball & Brown's empirical result contradicts the accounting theory view (prevalent in the 1960s) that accounting income is too distorted by arbitrary rules (depreciation methods, inventory valuation, revenue recognition) to be informative. The market uses accounting income â€” warts and all â€” as its primary information variable. This supports the EPS paradigm's reliance on reported EPS: managers maximize reported EPS because that's what investors use to price stocks.
