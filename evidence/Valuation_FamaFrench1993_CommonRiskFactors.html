Fama & French (1993): Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and Bonds

Fama, Eugene F., and Kenneth R. French. "Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and Bonds." *Journal of Financial Economics* 33, no. 1 (February 1993): 3-56.

Key Finding

Fama & French's 1993 JFE paper constructs the three-factor model for stock returns: R_i - RF = a_i + b_i(RM-RF) + s_i·SMB + h_i·HML + e_i, where SMB (Small Minus Big) captures the size premium and HML (High Minus Low) captures the value premium. The paper shows this three-factor model explains most of the cross-sectional variation in average returns on 25 size-BE/ME portfolios — intercepts are close to zero, meaning size and BE/ME factors capture risk priced by the market. The paper also identifies two bond-market factors — TERM (term premium) and DEF (default premium) — creating a unified five-factor framework linking stock and bond returns. This model became the standard benchmark for asset pricing, performance evaluation, and event studies for two decades.

Details

From Cross-Section to Time Series (pp. 3-5)

The 1992 paper showed that size and BE/ME explain the cross-section of average returns. This paper asks: are these patterns driven by common risk factors in returns? The approach shifts from Fama-MacBeth cross-sectional regressions to time-series regressions in the Black-Jensen-Scholes (1972) tradition.

If the three-factor model is correct, time-series regressions of portfolio excess returns on the three factors should produce intercepts (alphas) of zero.

Factor Construction (pp. 7-9)

Size factor — SMB (Small Minus Big):

  • Each June, stocks sorted into Small (below NYSE median ME) and Big (above)
  • SMB = average return on three small portfolios minus average return on three big portfolios
  • Average SMB return: 0.27%/month (t = 2.28)

Value factor — HML (High Minus Low):

  • Each June, stocks sorted by BE/ME into Low (bottom 30%), Medium (middle 40%), High (top 30%) using NYSE breakpoints
  • HML = average return on two high-BE/ME portfolios minus average return on two low-BE/ME portfolios
  • Average HML return: 0.40%/month (t = 2.91) ≈ 4.8%/year

Market factor — RM-RF:

  • Value-weighted return on all stocks minus one-month T-bill rate
  • Average: 0.43%/month (t = 1.76)

Bond factors:

  • TERM = long-term government bond return minus T-bill rate (term structure premium)
  • DEF = long-term corporate bond return minus long-term government bond return (default premium)

The 25 Test Portfolios (pp. 9-11)

Dependent variables: 25 portfolios formed from independent sorts on size (5 quintiles) × BE/ME (5 quintiles). These create a wide spread in average returns:

  • Smallest size, highest BE/ME: 1.72%/month
  • Largest size, lowest BE/ME: 0.89%/month
  • Spread provides a demanding test of the model

Stock-Market Results (Tables 3-6, pp. 14-24)

Market factor alone (CAPM):

  • Intercepts are large and systematically related to size and BE/ME
  • CAPM leaves 46-172 basis points/month unexplained
  • The CAPM fails dramatically for these portfolios

Three-factor model (RM-RF + SMB + HML):

  • Intercepts shrink dramatically — most are within 10-20 basis points of zero
  • R² values: 0.83 to 0.97
  • SMB loadings (s) decrease from small to big; HML loadings (h) increase from low to high BE/ME
  • The three-factor model "works" — it captures the size and value patterns that the CAPM misses

Key regression: For the smallest-size, highest-BE/ME portfolio:

R - RF = -0.02 + 1.03(RM-RF) + 1.46·SMB + 0.71·HML

The intercept of -0.02 (essentially zero) means the three factors explain the high average return of this extreme portfolio.

Bond-Market Results (Tables 7-8, pp. 24-31)

  • TERM and DEF explain time-series variation in bond returns
  • These bond factors also enter significantly in stock-return regressions (especially for small and low-grade firms)
  • Stock and bond markets share common risk factors through the term structure
  • When TERM and DEF are added to stock regressions, they are significant but do not displace SMB and HML

The Integrated Model (pp. 31-36)

The five-factor model for stocks:

R_i - RF = a_i + b_i(RM-RF) + s_i·SMB + h_i·HML + m_i·TERM + n_i·DEF + e_i

Stock and bond returns share common exposure to TERM and DEF, establishing a link between equity and fixed-income risk pricing. However, for stock portfolios, the three stock-market factors dominate.

Rational Risk Interpretation (pp. 4-5, 37-41)

Fama & French interpret the factors as state variables in an ICAPM (Merton 1973):

  • SMB and HML are mimicking portfolios for underlying risk factors related to size and distress
  • High BE/ME firms are financially distressed → their stocks are risky in ways beta doesn't capture
  • Small firms face greater uncertainty about future earnings → additional risk
  • The factors earn positive premiums because investors demand compensation for bearing these risks

This is the rational interpretation: the market is efficient, and the three-factor model captures risk that the CAPM misses.

Significance

1. The Standard Asset Pricing Model (1993-2015)

The Fama-French three-factor model became THE benchmark for empirical asset pricing. Every event study, performance evaluation, and anomaly test used it. Jensen's alpha (from CAPM) was replaced by Fama-French alpha (from the three-factor model). This was the standard for over two decades until Fama-French (2015) added profitability and investment factors.

2. Value and Size as "Risk Factors"

By constructing SMB and HML as tradeable factors and showing they explain the cross-section, Fama & French transformed the anomaly literature. What were previously "anomalies" (size effect, value effect) became "risk factors" — systematic sources of return that investors are compensated for bearing. Whether these truly represent risk or mispricing remains debated.

3. The Bridge Between APT and Practice

Ross's APT (1976) said multiple factors matter but didn't say which ones. Fama-French (1993) provided specific, empirically grounded factors. This made the APT practical and testable, bridging abstract theory and empirical implementation.

4. The Factor Zoo Problem

By demonstrating that ad hoc factors can explain cross-sectional returns, Fama-French opened the door to hundreds of proposed factors — momentum (Carhart 1997), profitability, investment, quality, low volatility, etc. Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016) documented 316 factors in the literature. The "factor zoo" is both a triumph of the Fama-French methodology and a cautionary tale about data mining.

5. Passive Factor Investing

The three-factor model showed that "alpha" from the CAPM was often just exposure to size and value factors. This led to factor-based investing strategies — "smart beta" — where investors could capture size and value premiums through passive, low-cost portfolios. DFA (Dimensional Fund Advisors), where Fama serves as director, commercialized this approach.

Paradigm

Academic

NPV / DCF: P = Σ CF/(1+r)^t. Cost of equity from CAPM / factor model. Objective = maximize firm value.

Era

The Great Divergence (1964–present)

Academic textbooks teach NPV/CAPM/DCF. Practitioners continue using EPS × P/E, earnings yields, and comps.

Connections

  • Built on: Fama-French (1992) cross-sectional evidence; Black-Jensen-Scholes (1972) time-series methodology; Merton (1973) ICAPM; Ross (1976) APT
  • Replaced: CAPM as the benchmark asset pricing model
  • Extended by: Carhart (1997) four-factor (+ momentum); Fama-French (2015) five-factor (+ profitability, investment)
  • Challenged by: Lakonishok, Shleifer & Vishny (1994) behavioral interpretation; Daniel & Titman (1997) characteristics vs. covariances
  • Commercialized by: DFA (Dimensional Fund Advisors) — factor-based passive investing
  • Formalized (inadvertently): Graham & Dodd value investing as a systematic risk factor
  • Stream: Pure academic — but with massive practical impact on asset management industry
