Gerstenberg (1924): Financial Organization and Management of Business

Gerstenberg, Charles W. *Financial Organization and Management of Business.* Prentice-Hall, 1924 (4th revised ed. 1959, read from 1960 Indian reprint).

Key Finding

Gerstenberg presents an explicit earnings capitalization formula (V = E/r, stated as "multiplier = 100/rate of return") for business valuation, a clear pre-M&M treatment of leverage ("trading on the equity") with numerical examples, and recognition of the tax advantage of debt — all within the descriptive-institutional framework that dominated corporate finance teaching from the 1920s-1950s. No present-value discounting, no DCF, no risk-return theory.

Details

Earnings Capitalization Formula (Ch. 9, p. 167)

"A business is worth what it will earn... its annual net earnings times an appropriate multiplier. The multiplier is 100% divided by the appropriate rate of return."

Example: Business earning $50,000/year, fair rate 8% → value = $625,000 ($50,000 / 0.08 = $50,000 × 12.5)

Practical case: Laundry earning $5,000/year. Seller wants $50,000 (10% return). Buyer thinks 10% too low given risk. Settle at $30,000 (16.7% return, ~6x earnings).

Two Theories of Capitalization

  1. Earnings theory (preferred): Value based on earning power. Multiplier reflects risk.
  2. Cost theory (rejected): Value based on cost of assets. Unsatisfactory because idle assets don't earn returns.

Trading on the Equity (pp. 173-174)

Clear numerical illustration of leverage effects:

  • $100K equity earning 10%, 15%, 3% in three years
  • Add $100K debt at 5%: returns become 15%, 25%, 1%
  • "In trading on the equity, gains are magnified; but so are losses"

Tax Advantage of Debt (p. 172-173)

"Bonds are always a lower-cost security to a corporation than stocks because... the corporation can deduct the interest paid on bonds as a business expense in arriving at its income tax, thus further reducing the cost of the capital."

Pre-M&M tax shield recognition, though treated as practical rather than theoretical.

Scientific Method for M&A Valuation (p. 467-468)

Two-component approach:

  • Tangible assets at appraised value → assigned to preferred stock
  • Excess earning power (capitalized earnings minus tangible assets) → assigned to common stock
  • Example: Tangible $100K, earnings $25K/yr at 10% = $250K capitalized, excess = $150K goodwill

Factor of Safety / Coverage Analysis (pp. 175-176)

Algebraic derivation: financial risk = (a - b) / a where a = normal revenue, b = worst-year decline. Interest coverage ratio as the key debt capacity metric.

What's NOT in the Book

  • No present-value or DCF analysis
  • No time value of money applied to equity
  • No systematic risk-return theory
  • No portfolio theory or market equilibrium
  • No Fisher influence visible despite Fisher (1907) predating first edition by 17 years

Significance

  1. The earnings multiplier was standard by the 1920s: V = E/r was well-established in corporate finance teaching, not new with Graham & Dodd
  2. Pre-M&M leverage analysis was clear and correct: The mechanics of leverage, including the tax advantage, were understood — what was missing was the theoretical framework showing when/whether leverage matters for total firm value
  3. Paradigmatic descriptive-institutional textbook: Shows what corporate finance looked like before the theoretical revolution
  4. Used for 35+ years: 4 editions (1924-1959) spanning the entire pre-modern era, making it one of the most influential corporate finance textbooks ever
  5. The V=E/r formula bridges practitioner and academic: Used in both M&A practice and academic teaching

Paradigm

Pre-Split

Before the 1958 revolution — everyone used the same framework.

Era

The Consensus Era (1720–1957)

For over 200 years, practitioners and scholars alike valued assets by capitalizing earnings at market-observed yields.

Connections

  • Same framework as: Lyon (1917), Mead (1910), Dewing (1920) — the first generation of corp fin textbooks
  • Explicitly uses the formula that: Bonbright (1937) would analyze in depth
  • Would be superseded by: Modigliani & Miller (1958), who showed that in perfect markets capital structure doesn't affect firm value
  • The "scientific method" for M&A foreshadows goodwill accounting and purchase price allocation in modern M&A
  • Compare with: Hutcheson (1720) used NAV/balance sheet; Gerstenberg uses earnings capitalization — documents the shift over 200 years
