Gordon (1959): Dividends, Earnings, and Stock Prices ‚Äî The Empirical Challenge to Earnings-Only Valuation

Gordon, Myron J. "Dividends, Earnings, and Stock Prices." *Review of Economics and Statistics* 41, no. 2, Part 1 (May 1959): 99-105.

Key Finding

Myron Gordon's 1959 Review of Economics and Statistics paper provides the first rigorous empirical test of whether stock prices depend on dividends AND earnings separately, or on earnings alone. Using cross-sectional regressions for four industries over two years (1951 and 1954), Gordon shows that dividends and retained earnings have DIFFERENT price multipliers ‚Äî with dividends consistently valued more highly than retained earnings. This rejects the "earnings hypothesis" (that only total earnings matter for price) in favor of the "dividend hypothesis" (that the division of earnings between dividends and retained earnings affects price). The paper provides the theoretical foundation: P‚ÇÄ = (1-b)Y‚ÇÄ/(k-br), where b is the retention rate and r is the return on reinvested earnings. The earnings hypothesis requires that k (the required rate of profit) be independent of dividend policy ‚Äî but Gordon's data reject this. For the EPS project, this paper is critical because it tests the very premise of P = E/k: if dividends matter separately from earnings, then the simple earnings capitalization formula is incomplete.

The Theoretical Framework (pp. 102-104)

Gordon derives the pricing formula from first principles:

P‚ÇÄ = D‚ÇÅ/(k - g) = (1-b)Y‚ÇÄ/(k - br)

where:

  • b = fraction of earnings retained (retention rate)
  • r = rate of return on reinvested earnings
  • k = rate of profit required by investors (cost of equity)
  • g = br = growth rate of dividends and earnings

The earnings hypothesis requires that k be independent of b (the retention/dividend rate). If k doesn't depend on how earnings are split between dividends and retention, then the division doesn't affect price, and only total earnings matter.

Gordon argues this fails because:

"An investor buying a share of stock is acquiring a share of a corporation's future earnings. What can he do with these future earnings? He can (a) receive them as dividends or (b) receive them through sale of stock."

"However, when he sells the stock, the buyer is also acquiring a share of the corporation's future earnings... We therefore have an infinite regress ‚Äî and the limit of which is the dividend series."

This is the "bird in the hand" argument: dividends are certain and immediate; retention promises uncertain future returns. Therefore investors should discount retained earnings more heavily (require higher k when b is higher).

The Industries Selected (p. 100)

Gordon deliberately chooses industries with different characteristics:

  • Chemicals: High growth, high retention
  • Foods: Stable, high payout
  • Steels: Cyclical
  • Machine Tools: Highly cyclical

The fact that dividends matter across ALL industry types strengthens the result ‚Äî it's not an artifact of one industry's peculiarities.

Significance

1. A Direct Test of P = E √ó (P/E)

The EPS paradigm's pricing model ‚Äî P = E(EPS) √ó P/E ‚Äî implies that only the LEVEL of earnings matters for price, with the P/E ratio determined by comparables. Gordon's paper tests this directly (his "Model III: earnings only") and finds it incomplete. The division between dividends and retained earnings matters. This is a challenge the EPS paradigm must address: if the payout ratio affects price, then P/E ratios are not solely determined by comps but also by payout policy.

2. But the Earnings Yield Dominates

Despite rejecting the pure earnings hypothesis, Gordon's R¬≤ values for the earnings-only model are still very high (Table 1 footnotes suggest R¬≤ around .70-.85 for earnings alone). Earnings explain most of the cross-sectional variation in prices; dividends provide incremental explanatory power. The EPS paradigm is not wrong ‚Äî it's a first-order approximation that captures most of the variation.

3. The P/E Ratio Depends on Payout ‚Äî Which Managers Know

If P/E = f(payout ratio), then EPS-maximizing managers face a portfolio of tools: they can boost EPS (which raises E in P = E √ó P/E) but they must also manage the payout ratio to maintain or increase the P/E multiple. This creates a nuanced optimization: maximize EPS while maintaining a payout ratio that supports the multiple. This is exactly what practitioners do ‚Äî manage "earnings quality" and "shareholder returns" simultaneously.

4. Gordon's Formula Is the Earnings Capitalization Formula with Growth

P‚ÇÄ = (1-b)Y‚ÇÄ/(k-br) is the Gordon Growth Model (GGM). When b = 0 (all earnings paid as dividends), P‚ÇÄ = Y‚ÇÄ/k ‚Äî the pure earnings capitalization formula. Growth (br > 0) modifies it. The EPS paradigm's P = E/r is the GGM with b = 0 or equivalently with the growth effect folded into r.

5. The "Bird in Hand" Is a Practitioner Argument

Gordon's reasoning ‚Äî that dividends are valued more because they're certain ‚Äî is the practitioner's argument, not the academic's. MM (1961) will argue this is a fallacy (the "bird in the hand" fallacy). The debate between Gordon and MM on dividends parallels the debate between practitioners and academics on the role of earnings vs. cash flows.

6. Cross-Sectional Evidence = Comparables

Gordon's methodology ‚Äî comparing P, D, and Y across firms within an industry ‚Äî IS the comparables approach. He's showing that within an industry, price varies systematically with dividends and earnings. This is what practitioners do when they apply P/E multiples: compare firms within an industry and adjust for payout and growth differences.

Paradigm

Practitioner

Earnings capitalization: P = EPS √ó P/E from comps. Cost of equity = earnings yield. Objective = maximize EPS.

Era

The Revolution (1958‚Äì1963)

In just five years, Modigliani-Miller, Markowitz, Sharpe, and Solomon replaced earnings maximization with value maximization as the academic standard.

Connections

  • Miller & Modigliani (1961) ‚Äî MM's dividend irrelevance theorem directly contradicts Gordon's findings. MM argue that in perfect markets, dividend policy doesn't affect value; Gordon's data show dividends are valued more than retained earnings.
  • Gordon & Shapiro (1956) ‚Äî The earlier Gordon-Shapiro paper derives the constant-growth formula P = D/(k-g), which this paper extends and tests empirically.
  • Lintner (1956) ‚Äî Lintner's finding that firms smooth dividends implies that the "average dividend rate" (dÃÑ) in Gordon's refined model captures the firm's TARGET payout, not random variation.
  • Graham & Dodd (1934/1940) ‚Äî Graham & Dodd's valuation formula P = M(D + E/3) = M √ó (4D/3 + E/3... actually D + E/3) places more weight on dividends than earnings ‚Äî the same asymmetry Gordon documents empirically.
  • Williams (1938) ‚Äî Williams's dividend discount model (V = sum of discounted future dividends) is the theoretical foundation for Gordon's "bird in the hand" argument that dividends are the ultimate source of value.
  • Solomon (1963) ‚Äî Solomon acknowledges that dividends affect price in the "traditional view" and that this is one area where MM's theoretical conclusions may not hold empirically.
  • Cragg & Malkiel (1982) ‚Äî Their later cross-sectional study of how growth expectations affect P/E ratios extends Gordon's work by using analyst forecasts rather than historical data.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS) ‚Äî Gordon's paper challenges the pure EPS paradigm (P = E √ó P/E) by showing payout matters. But it also supports it: earnings are the DOMINANT factor (R¬≤ = .86-.93 with dividends), and the division between D and Y-D is a second-order refinement.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS and dividends) ‚Äî Gordon's evidence that dividends are valued more than retained earnings implies that EPS-maximizing managers face a trade-off: retaining earnings to fund EPS-accretive investments vs. paying dividends to support the P/E multiple. The dividend paper should address this.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (maxEPS paradigm) ‚Äî Gordon (1959) represents the last major pre-MM challenge to earnings-only valuation. After MM (1961) and the rise of CAPM, the academic consensus shifted to viewing dividends as irrelevant. But Gordon's empirical finding persists in the data.
