Smith (1924): Common Stocks as Long Term Investments

Smith, Edgar Lawrence. *Common Stocks as Long Term Investments.* New York: Macmillan, 1924.

Key Finding

Edgar Lawrence Smith's 1924 book is the first known systematic empirical demonstration of the equity premium â€” that diversified common stocks outperform bonds over all long periods, whether the dollar is appreciating or depreciating. His key analytical insight: corporate retained earnings, reinvested at a positive return, compound equity value like compound interest at ~2.5-3.2% p.a. This "retained earnings = compound interest" idea directly anticipates the Gordon Growth Model and the sustainable growth rate formula by decades.

Details

Core Argument

  • Set out to prove bonds beat stocks during deflationary periods (1866-1899) â€” the data refused to cooperate
  • 11 separate tests comparing $10,000 in diversified stocks vs. $10,000 in 4% bonds
  • Stocks won in 10 of 11 tests, across inflationary AND deflationary periods
  • Total advantage of stocks: ranged from -$1,012 (worst case) to +$21,955 (best case)

Valuation Approach

  • No formal valuation model (no DCF, no capitalization of earnings, no P/E ratio)
  • Uses dividend yield as stock selection screen in some tests
  • Compares total returns (income + capital appreciation) empirically
  • Introduces purchasing-power-adjusted returns using wholesale price indices (early real return analysis)

The Retained Earnings Insight (Chapter IX, pp. 76-80)

"Over a period of years these directors will never aim to declare all the company's net earnings in dividends. They will turn back a part of such earnings to surplus account, and invest this increasing surplus in productive operation. Such a policy successfully carried out is in fact a practical demonstration of the principle of compound interest."

This is the conceptual ancestor of:

  • g = ROE Ã— retention ratio (sustainable growth rate)
  • The Gordon Growth Model: P = D/(r-g)
  • The Buffett concept of "owner earnings" compounding

Two Laws (p. 79)

  1. "The principal value of a well diversified holding of common stocks tends to increase in accordance with the operation of compound interest"
  2. "Such stock holdings may be relied upon to pay an average income return of something more than the average current rate on commercial paper"

Other Notable Innovations

  • Mechanical stock selection: Uses non-judgmental rules (most-traded stocks, highest dividend yield) to eliminate hindsight bias â€” proto-index-fund logic
  • Diversification as prerequisite: "Without diversification, the purchase of common stocks cannot be considered"
  • Time-hazard analysis: Calculates probability of loss recovery by holding period (63% no loss in year 1; 94.4% recovery within 4 years)
  • Human factor: Management's incentives are aligned with equity, not debt â€” an early agency theory observation

Impact

  • Keynes (1925) reviewed it and singled out the retained-earnings insight as the key discovery
  • Credited with helping fuel the late-1920s bull market by legitimizing stock investment
  • Post-crash critics blamed Smith's thesis for encouraging speculation

Significance

  1. First known equity premium study: Systematic empirical evidence that stocks beat bonds long-term
  2. Growth through reinvestment: The retained-earnings-as-compound-interest insight is the conceptual bridge between yield-based valuation and growth-based valuation
  3. Practitioner stream: Written for investors, not academics â€” yet more rigorous than most academic work of the era
  4. Radical for its time: In 1924, "investment" meant bonds; "speculation" meant stocks. Smith challenged this orthodoxy with data.
  5. Real returns: Early use of inflation-adjusted return comparisons
