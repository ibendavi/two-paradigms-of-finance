Fama (1965): The Behavior of Stock-Market Prices â€” Random Walks and Fat Tails

Fama, Eugene F. "The Behavior of Stock-Market Prices." *Journal of Business* 38, no. 1 (January 1965): 34-105.

Key Finding

Fama's 1965 Journal of Business paper (his doctoral dissertation) provides the first comprehensive empirical test of the random walk model for stock prices. Two major results: (1) successive price changes are effectively independent â€” chart reading and technical analysis have no value; (2) the distribution of price changes has fat tails consistent with Mandelbrot's stable Paretian hypothesis (characteristic exponent Î± < 2), meaning the Gaussian assumption is wrong and variance may be infinite. The paper also contains the proto-EMH argument: "an efficient market" is one where "actual prices at every point in time represent very good estimates of intrinsic values." This paper, along with Fama (1970), would define the efficient markets research program for a generation.

Details

The Random Walk Model (pp. 35-40)

The random walk model requires two hypotheses:

  1. Independence: Successive price changes are independent random variables
  2. Identical distribution: Price changes conform to some probability distribution

Fama emphasizes that the random walk model is really a market theory, not just a statistical observation:

"An 'efficient' market is defined as a market where there are large numbers of rational, profit-maximizers actively competing, with each trying to predict future market values of individual securities, and where important current information is almost freely available to all participants." (p. 56)

In such a market, competition ensures that prices "at any point in time will be very good estimates of intrinsic values" and price changes will be "independent identically distributed random variables."

Mandelbrot's Stable Paretian Hypothesis (pp. 40-55)

The distribution of stock returns has:

  • Fat tails: Extreme price changes occur far more often than the normal distribution predicts
  • High peaks: More observations near the center than Gaussian

These features are consistent with Mandelbrot's stable Paretian distributions with characteristic exponent Î± < 2 (where Î± = 2 is Gaussian). Fama estimates Î± â‰ˆ 1.7 for most DJIA stocks.

Critical implication: If Î± < 2, the variance is infinite (does not exist in a population sense). This would undermine:

  • All mean-variance portfolio theory (Markowitz)
  • Standard statistical tests based on sample means and variances
  • The law of large numbers in its usual form

"If the population variance of the distribution of first differences is infinite, the sample variance is a meaningless measure of the dispersion." (p. 46)

Empirical Tests of Independence (pp. 55-73)

Data: Daily prices for 30 DJIA stocks, 1957-1962 (~1,200 observations each).

Serial correlation tests (pp. 64-66):

  • Mean serial correlation at lag 1: +0.026 (for daily changes)
  • Largest: +0.112 (Alcoa); most are tiny
  • Conclusion: "dependence in day-to-day price changes is either extremely slight or non-existent"

Runs tests (pp. 66-69):

  • Count sequences of positive/negative changes
  • Results: actual runs close to expected under independence
  • Slight tendency toward too few runs (positive serial dependence), consistent with serial correlations

Filter rules (pp. 69-73):

  • Alexander's (1961) strategy: buy when price rises x% from a trough, sell when it falls x% from a peak
  • For small filters (0.5%, 1.0%): profits exist but vanish after transaction costs
  • For larger filters (2%+): buy-and-hold outperforms
  • "There is no evidence that the filter technique, based on knowledge of the past behavior of prices, can make profits greater than buy-and-hold." (p. 73)

Mutual Fund Performance (pp. 73-78)

Reviews evidence on professional fund managers:

  • If independence holds, "the work of the chartist is of no real value in stock market analysis"
  • Even fundamental analysts face an uphill battle: in an efficient market, prices already reflect available information
  • Available evidence suggests mutual funds do not consistently outperform the market

The EMH Argument (p. 56)

The core proto-EMH reasoning:

"If the random walk model is a valid description of reality, the work of the chartist, like that of the astrologer, is of no real value in stock market analysis."

But Fama goes further â€” if fundamental analysis were consistently successful, competition among analysts would drive prices to intrinsic values, making even fundamental analysis a zero-sum game in the aggregate.

Significance

1. The Empirical Foundation for EMH

This paper provided the data. Fama (1970) would provide the theory. Together they constitute the empirical-theoretical foundation for the efficient markets hypothesis. The 1965 paper is the more ambitious empirical effort â€” 73 pages of careful statistical testing.

2. The Fat Tails Challenge

Fama's evidence for Î± < 2 (infinite variance) was a direct challenge to Markowitz's mean-variance framework. If variance doesn't exist, portfolio optimization based on variance is meaningless. This tension was never fully resolved â€” practice adopted the Gaussian assumption, while the data continued to show fat tails. The 2008 financial crisis would dramatically illustrate the real-world cost of ignoring fat tails.

3. The Death of Technical Analysis

The random walk evidence â€” serial correlations near zero, runs tests consistent with independence, filter rules unprofitable after costs â€” provided the first rigorous basis for dismissing technical analysis. This remains one of the most robust findings in empirical finance.

4. Proto-Behavioral Finance

Paradoxically, Fama's fat tails evidence (which he attributed to Mandelbrot's stable distributions) could alternatively be explained by behavioral phenomena â€” overreaction, herding, panic. The same data that supported EMH also contained the seeds of its critique.
