Dichev, Graham, Harvey & Rajgopal (2013): Earnings Quality â€” Evidence from the Field

Dichev, Ilia D., John R. Graham, Campbell R. Harvey, and Shiva Rajgopal. "Earnings Quality: Evidence from the Field." *Journal of Accounting and Economics* 56, no. 2-3 (2013): 1-33. (Dichev at Emory University; Graham at Duke University; Harvey at Duke University; Rajgopal at Emory University.)

Key Finding

Dichev, Graham, Harvey & Rajgopal's 2013 Journal of Accounting and Economics paper surveys 169 public and 206 private company CFOs (plus 12 in-depth interviews) about earnings quality. The central finding for the EPS project: CFOs overwhelmingly view earnings as a number whose primary purpose is VALUATION â€” 94.67% rate "for use by investors in valuing the company" as a very important use of earnings (average rating 4.72 out of 5), ranking first among all uses. CFOs view earnings as "one number" used for both external and internal purposes. High-quality earnings are defined as "sustainable, repeatable, recurring" (the #1 response in both public and private firms) and "free from one-time items" (#2). About 50% of earnings quality is determined by innate factors (business model, industry, macro conditions) rather than managerial discretion. On earnings management: CFOs estimate that roughly 20% of firms misrepresent economic performance in any given year, with the typical magnitude approximately 10% of reported EPS (9.85 cents per dollar of EPS). The top motivations for earnings management are: influencing stock price (93.5% agree), outside pressure to hit earnings benchmarks (92.9%), inside pressure to hit benchmarks (91.0%), and influencing executive compensation (88.6%). Reporting discretion has declined over the past 20 years, and CFOs view financial reporting as increasingly a compliance exercise. CFOs strongly prefer matching-based accounting (92.2%) and conservatism (75.4%) over fair value accounting. For the EPS project, this paper provides the CFO-side confirmation that earnings are understood primarily as a VALUATION input â€” the "one number" that determines what the company is worth â€” exactly as P = EPS x P/E predicts.

Details

Section 1: Introduction (pp. 1-3)

Motivation: Despite enormous academic literature on earnings quality, there is little direct evidence from the practitioners who produce and use earnings. This paper surveys and interviews CFOs to understand how they think about earnings quality â€” its definition, determinants, and vulnerability to manipulation.

Three sets of findings:

  1. The concept and characteristics of earnings quality
  2. Standard setting and its impact on earnings quality
  3. The prevalence and detection of earnings management

Section 2: Survey Design (pp. 3-5)

Sample: 558 total responses (5.4% response rate). 169 public firms, 206 private firms. Demographics: manufacturing dominant, MBA majority, ~49% corporate finance background. Also 12 in-depth CFO interviews and 2 standard setter interviews.

Section 5.5: Interview Insights (pp. 23-24)

Audit firm behavior: CFOs complain that FASB's rules-based approach reduces auditor judgment. "The big audit firms are not passing authority downstream to the regional headquarters." Junior auditors lack training. "The rules in the accounting firms come from high above now rather than from the field."

Reporting as compliance: Several CFOs view financial reporting as a "compliance activity" â€” "we just do what the regulators tell us to do rather than innovate via reporting practices for better access to capital." This suggests that reporting has shifted from an information-provision tool to a regulatory checkbox.

Several CFOs specifically mention acquisition accounting: "I have seen some CFOs taking advantage. Section 7: Conclusions (pp. 30-31)

"Most respondents believe that high quality earnings are sustainable and are free from one-time items. They add that high quality earnings reflect consistent reporting choices, are backed by actual cash flows, and avoid unreliable long-term estimates."

"CFO views on earnings quality remain largely the same across public and private firms, for different uses of earnings, and over various manager and firm characteristics. The dominance of the sustainability notion of earnings quality, combined with CFO preference for 'one number' earnings for external and internal uses, suggests that earnings quality is a more homogenous and stable concept than previously thought."

Significance

1. Earnings Are Understood as a VALUATION Input â€” By the People Who Produce Them

The finding that 94.67% of CFOs rate "for use by investors in valuing the company" as the most important use of earnings is direct evidence that CFOs understand the P = EPS x P/E model. They produce earnings primarily so investors can value the company â€” exactly as the EPS paradigm describes. This is the supply-side confirmation of investor-side pricing.

2. "One Number" Confirms EPS as the Objective Function

CFOs view earnings as a single number serving all purposes â€” valuation, contracting, compensation, management. This "one number" view is precisely the EPS paradigm's claim: EPS is not one of many metrics but THE metric that governs corporate behavior. The fact that CFOs don't distinguish between earnings-for-valuation and earnings-for-compensation confirms that EPS maximization is the unified objective.

3. Sustainability = Earnings Capitalization

The #1 definition of high-quality earnings â€” "sustainable, repeatable, recurring" â€” is exactly what makes earnings suitable for capitalization. In P = EPS x P/E, the EPS that matters is sustainable EPS. CFOs define earnings quality in terms that validate the capitalization model: good earnings are earnings you can capitalize.

4. The Top Motivations for Earnings Management Are All EPS-Related

Stock price (93.5%), benchmark pressure (92.9% outside, 91.0% inside), and compensation (88.6%) â€” ALL of these operate through EPS. The stock price depends on EPS (via P = EPS x P/E). The benchmarks ARE EPS targets. The compensation is tied to EPS (Huang et al. 2014). Every motivation points to EPS maximization.

5. ~10% of EPS Is Managed â€” A Quantitative Estimate

The finding that typical misrepresentation is ~10 cents per dollar of EPS gives a concrete magnitude to earnings management. This is not trivial â€” 10% of EPS translates to 10% of stock price under P = EPS x P/E. It confirms that EPS management is economically significant.

6. Acquisition Accounting Confirms the M&A EPS Connection

CFOs explicitly identify acquisition accounting as a vehicle for earnings management â€” setting up reserves at acquisition that are later released into earnings. This connects to Andrade (1997) and Dasgupta, Harford & Ma (2024): EPS concerns shape not only which deals get done and how they're structured but also how they're accounted for post-completion.

7. Matching Preference Over Fair Value Supports EPS Stability

The overwhelming preference for matching (92.2%) over fair value reflects the desire for earnings that are smooth, predictable, and capitalizable. Fair value accounting introduces mark-to-market volatility that makes earnings harder to capitalize (the P/E multiple becomes unstable). CFOs want the accounting system that produces the most capitalizable earnings.

8. Declining Discretion Paradox

CFOs say reporting discretion has declined â€” yet 20% of firms still manage earnings. The decline in discretion may push earnings management toward REAL actions (cutting R&D, capex, employment) rather than accounting choices. This connects to Almeida, Fos & Kronlund (2016): when accrual management becomes harder, real earnings management (via repurchases, investment cuts) becomes more important.
