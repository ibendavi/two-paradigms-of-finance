The Two Paradigms

Every concept in corporate finance has two versions: the one in the textbook and the one in the boardroom. Here they are, side by side, with evidence.

The Academic Paradigm (post-1958) derives from Modigliani-Miller, CAPM, and efficient markets theory. The Practitioner Paradigm is the older framework â€” earnings capitalization, market multiples, EPS maximization â€” that survived the revolution unchanged. Neither is "wrong." They are different objective functions with different inputs, producing different answers.

Key Insight

The Academic View

Practitioners who use EPS are making a mistake. They confuse the earnings-price ratio with the market capitalization rate (Brealey & Myers). They fall for the "bootstrap game" in M&A. They need to be educated out of these errors.

"Managers frequently make poor financial decisions because they confuse earnings-price ratios with the market capitalization rate."
â€” Brealey & Myers, Ch. 4

The EPS Paradigm View

Practitioners who use EPS are following a different objective function, not making a mistake. EPS maximization is internally consistent and historically predates NPV by centuries. It's a robust business method, not a behavioral bias.

"Earnings are in a class by themselves. Cash flows are nowhere near as important as earnings."
â€” CFO interview, Graham, Harvey & Rajgopal 2005

The academic paradigm needs many frictions (taxes, information asymmetry, agency costs, behavioral biases) to explain why firms do what they do â€” because the frictionless baseline (MM) predicts nothing should matter. The EPS paradigm generates predictions without frictions: leverage exists because debt is cheaper than the earnings yield; buybacks exist because retiring shares boosts EPS; M&A is evaluated by EPS accretion.
