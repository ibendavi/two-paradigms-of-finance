The Split

In just five years (1958â€“1963), academic finance was transformed. The objective function changed from "maximize earnings" to "maximize value." But practitioners never made the switch.

Before: The Consensus (pre-1958)

For generations, finance textbooks taught essentially the same framework. The firm's goal was to maximize the earning power of shareholders' capital. The cost of equity was the earnings yield (E/P) â€” an observable market rate. The value of a stock was earnings capitalized at a rate from comparables.

The Old Framework

  • Objective: Maximize earning power (EPS)
  • Cost of equity: Earnings yield (E/P ~10%)
  • Valuation: V = Earnings / Cap rate (from comps)
  • Leverage: "Trading on equity" â€” use cheap debt to boost EPS
  • Decision rule: Does this action increase earnings per share?

â€” Taught by Mead (1910), Dewing (1920), Gerstenberg (1924), Graham & Dodd (1934), Guthmann & Dougall (1940), Dean (1951), Weston (1962)

The New Framework

  • Objective: Maximize net present value (firm value)
  • Cost of equity: CAPM: Rf + β × (Rm - Rf)
  • Valuation: P = Σ CFt / (1+r)t
  • Leverage: Irrelevant without frictions (MM)
  • Decision rule: Does this project have positive NPV?

â€” Established by MM (1958), Markowitz (1952/59), Solomon (1963), Sharpe (1964), Brealey & Myers (1981+)

The Five-Year Revolution

1952/1959 â€” Markowitz: Risk Gets Mathematics

Harry Markowitz introduces mean-variance portfolio theory. For the first time, risk isn't just a qualitative judgment â€” it's a number (variance, covariance). This is the mathematical foundation that will eventually produce CAPM and a theoretically derived cost of equity.

1958 â€” Modigliani & Miller: Capital Structure Irrelevance

The bombshell. MM prove â€” by arbitrage â€” that the total value of a firm is independent of how it's financed. Leverage doesn't create value; it just reshuffles risk between debt and equity.

But this is only true if the firm's goal is to maximize total firm value. Under the old paradigm â€” where the goal is to maximize EPS â€” leverage obviously matters: debt at 3% replaces equity at 10% (the earnings yield), mechanically boosting EPS. MM's irrelevance result implicitly assumes away the EPS objective.

1961 â€” Miller & Modigliani: Dividend Irrelevance

The companion result: dividend policy is also irrelevant. Only investment policy matters. Under perfect markets, a dollar of dividends is exactly offset by a dollar of capital loss.

But practitioners had always cared about dividends â€” stable, growing dividends were the hallmark of a well-managed firm. MM's theorem says this is irrational. Practitioners disagree. The divergence deepens.

1962 â€” Weston: The Rosetta Stone

Weston writes: "Economic theory prescribes the rule that the investor maximize the present worth of future earnings... The scare quotes around "practical" are telling. Weston sees the conflict. He knows managers use EPS. He can't quite bring himself to call it wrong. But the writing is on the wall.

After: Two Worlds

After 1963, academic textbooks universally adopt value maximization. Brealey & Myers (1981+) devotes three separate chapters to demolishing EPS-based reasoning. The CAPM (Sharpe 1964) provides a theoretically derived cost of equity, replacing the observable earnings yield.

But practitioners never switched. Surveys of CFOs (Graham, Harvey & Rajgopal 2005) show that managers still maximize EPS, still use earnings yields, still evaluate M&A by EPS accretion/dilution. Damodaran (2012) admits "most valuations done in the real world are relative valuations" using P/E multiples.

The academy tells practitioners they're wrong. Practitioners tell the academy they're irrelevant. The two paradigms have coexisted â€” uneasily â€” for over 60 years.
