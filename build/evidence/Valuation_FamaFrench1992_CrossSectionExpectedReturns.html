Fama & French (1992): The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns — Beta Is Dead

Fama, Eugene F., and Kenneth R. French. "The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns." *Journal of Finance* 47, no. 2 (June 1992): 427-465.

Key Finding

Fama & French's 1992 Journal of Finance paper delivers the most devastating empirical blow to the CAPM: beta has NO reliable relation to average stock returns for 1963-1990. Two variables — size (market equity, ME) and book-to-market equity (BE/ME) — capture the cross-sectional variation in average returns. In Fama-MacBeth regressions, the beta slope is 0.15 with a t-statistic of 0.46 (essentially zero), while ln(BE/ME) has a slope of 0.50 with t = 5.71 and ln(ME) has a slope of -0.15 with t = -2.58. When size and BE/ME are included, beta adds nothing, leverage adds nothing, and E/P adds nothing. The paper concludes: "our tests do not support the most basic prediction of the SLB model, that average stock returns are positively related to market βs." This paper, along with Fama-French (1993), redirected asset pricing from single-factor to multi-factor models.

Details

The CAPM Under Test (pp. 427-428)

The Sharpe-Lintner-Black (SLB) model predicts:

E(R_i) = RF + β_i · [E(R_M) - RF]

Only market beta should explain the cross-section of expected returns. This is the prediction tested and rejected.

Data and Method (pp. 429-432)

  • NYSE, AMEX, NASDAQ stocks, 1963-1990
  • Monthly returns on portfolios formed on size, pre-ranking beta, and other variables
  • Fama-MacBeth (1973) cross-sectional regressions: monthly regressions of returns on characteristics, then time-series average of slopes
  • 100 portfolios formed on size (10 deciles) × pre-ranking beta (10 deciles within each size decile)

The Death of Beta (Table I, pp. 433-436)

100 portfolios sorted on size and pre-ranking beta:

  • Within size deciles, there is NO reliable relation between beta and average return
  • The apparent beta-return relation in the unconditional data is entirely due to the correlation between beta and size
  • Post-ranking betas range from 0.5 to 1.8, but average returns within size deciles are flat across beta groups
  • "In contrast to the SLB model, the relation between β and average return for 1963-1990 is weak" (p. 436)

Size Effect (Table II, pp. 436-438)

  • Average monthly returns decline monotonically from the smallest to largest size decile
  • Smallest decile: 1.47%/month; largest: 0.90%/month
  • The size effect is robust and not explained by beta

Book-to-Market Equity Effect (Table IV, pp. 441-443)

  • Average returns increase sharply from low to high BE/ME portfolios
  • Lowest BE/ME decile: 0.30%/month; highest: 1.83%/month
  • The spread is enormous — 1.53%/month (≈18.4%/year)
  • BE/ME is the single most powerful variable for explaining cross-sectional returns

The Fama-MacBeth Regressions (Table III, pp. 439-441)

The key result table:

Variable Slope t-stat
β alone 0.15 0.46
ln(ME) alone -0.15 -2.58
ln(BE/ME) alone 0.50 5.71
β + ln(ME) + ln(BE/ME) β: -0.11, ME: -0.11, BE/ME: 0.35 β: -0.35, ME: -1.99, BE/ME: 4.44

Beta's t-statistic of 0.46 is the death sentence for the CAPM. Adding beta to size and BE/ME contributes nothing.

Other Variables Subsumed (pp. 443-445)

  • Leverage: The leverage effect (Bhandari 1988) is absorbed by BE/ME
  • E/P (earnings/price): The E/P effect (Basu 1983) is absorbed by BE/ME and size
  • BE/ME is the dominant variable — it subsumes the explanatory power of leverage and E/P

Two Interpretations (pp. 427-428, 444-445)

Fama & French acknowledge two possible interpretations:

  1. Rational risk: Size and BE/ME proxy for state variables in the ICAPM (Merton 1973) or APT (Ross 1976) — they capture risk not measured by market beta
  2. Irrational: BE/ME captures market overreaction — the market overprices glamour stocks and underprices distressed/value stocks

The paper does not resolve this — "whether the patterns in returns are the result of rational or irrational asset pricing is an open question."

Sub-Period Analysis (pp. 435-436)

  • Pre-1963 (the SLB-supporting period): the beta-return relation exists but is due to size
  • 1963-1990: no beta-return relation at all
  • 1981-1990: the beta-return relation is actually slightly negative

Significance

1. The CAPM Dethroned

This paper did more than any other to end the CAPM's dominance as the workhorse asset pricing model. The finding that beta is useless for explaining cross-sectional returns was a bombshell. After 1992, the CAPM could no longer be used uncritically in empirical work.

2. Book-to-Market as the Central Variable

BE/ME emerged as the single most powerful predictor of cross-sectional stock returns. This was both an empirical discovery and a puzzle — WHY does BE/ME predict returns? The rational/irrational debate remains unresolved.

3. Value Investing Validated (Inadvertently)

High BE/ME stocks earn higher average returns than low BE/ME stocks. This is exactly what Graham & Dodd practitioners had argued for decades — buy cheap stocks (high book-to-market) and avoid expensive ones. Fama & French provided the most rigorous empirical confirmation of value investing, even though they did not cite Graham & Dodd and framed it as a risk story rather than a mispricing story.

4. The Multi-Factor Revolution

By showing that beta alone is insufficient, Fama & French opened the door to multi-factor models — first their own three-factor model (1993), then Carhart (1997) four-factor, then Fama-French (2015) five-factor. The entire "factor zoo" literature traces to this paper.

5. Fama's Elegant Dilemma

Eugene Fama — the architect of the efficient markets hypothesis — produced the most damaging evidence against the CAPM, which is itself an efficient-markets model. He resolved this by arguing that BE/ME proxies for rational risk, not mispricing. Critics (Lakonishok, Shleifer & Vishny 1994) argued it reflects irrational overreaction. This debate defines modern asset pricing.
