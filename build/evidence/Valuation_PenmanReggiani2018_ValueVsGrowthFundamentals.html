Penman & Reggiani (2018): Fundamentals of Value vs. Growth Investing and an Explanation for the Value Trap

Penman, Stephen H., and Francesco Reggiani. "Fundamentals of Value vs. Growth Investing and an Explanation for the Value Trap." *Financial Analysts Journal* (forthcoming 2018). SSRN Working Paper, July 2018. (Penman was at Columbia University; Reggiani was at Bocconi University.)

Key Finding

Penman & Reggiani's 2018 Financial Analysts Journal paper (SSRN working paper July 2018, earlier title "The Value Trap: Value Buys Risky Growth") provides a unified framework connecting the earnings yield (E/P), the book-to-price ratio (B/P), return on equity (ROE), expected returns (r), and expected earnings growth (g). Starting from P₀ = Earnings₁/(r - g), they derive E/P = r - g (equation 1a) and B/P = (1/ROE) × (r - g) (equation 2). The central insight: for a given E/P (i.e., for a given r - g), B/P sorts firms by inverse ROE — high B/P means low ROE, which under conservative accounting indicates HIGH expected earnings growth and HIGH risk. Thus "value" investing (buying high B/P stocks) does not buy safe cheapness — it buys risky growth. This is the "value trap": value stocks appear cheap but actually bear growth risk that justifies their high expected returns. Using NYSE/AMEX/NASDAQ data 1963-2015 (176,848 firm-years), double sorts on E/P × B/P show that mean returns range from 1.9% (low E/P, low B/P) to 27.1% (high E/P, high B/P), and B/P is positively associated with subsequent earnings growth and earnings volatility for given E/P. For the EPS project, this paper is essential because it shows that the earnings yield (E/P) IS the pricing variable that determines expected returns, with B/P serving merely as a proxy for growth expectations conditional on E/P.

Details

The Framework: E/P = r - g (pp. 2-6)

Starting from the standard constant-growth model:

P₀ = Earnings₁/(r - g)

Rearranging:

E/P = r - g (Equation 1a)

The earnings yield equals the expected return minus the expected earnings growth rate. This is a tautology given the constant-growth assumption, but it has powerful implications:

  • E/P does NOT equal r (the expected return) unless g = 0
  • E/P understates r when growth is positive
  • E/P overstates r when growth is negative (earnings decline)
  • Two firms with the same E/P can have very different r and g, as long as r - g is the same

B/P as a Function of E/P and ROE (pp. 5-7)

Multiply and divide:

B/P = (Book Value₀/Earnings₁) × (Earnings₁/P₀) = (1/ROE) × E/P

Therefore:

B/P = (1/ROE) × (r - g) (Equation 2)

This decomposes B/P into two components: the inverse of profitability (1/ROE) and the earnings yield (r - g).

Three Properties for Given E/P (pp. 7-9)

For a fixed level of E/P (i.e., fixed r - g):

(i) B/P is determined by the inverse of ROE. Higher B/P ↔ lower ROE.

(ii) If lower ROE indicates higher expected earnings growth (which conservative accounting implies), then higher B/P → higher expected g.

(iii) If higher B/P → higher g, and E/P = r - g is fixed, then higher B/P → higher r (expected return). Higher growth must be accompanied by higher required return to keep E/P constant.

The Conservative Accounting Mechanism (pp. 8-11)

Why does low ROE signal high expected growth? Under conservative (GAAP) accounting:

  • Earnings recognition is deferred until uncertainty is resolved
  • R&D, advertising, brand-building expenses are written off immediately (not capitalized)
  • This depresses current book value and current earnings for firms with significant growth investments
  • BUT these investments generate future earnings growth
  • Therefore: low current ROE (due to conservative accounting) → high expected future earnings growth

This is NOT a market inefficiency — it is an accounting measurement property. Conservative accounting creates a mechanical link between low current profitability and high future growth.

Table 1: Double Sort Returns — E/P × B/P (pp. 12-14)

176,848 firm-years, NYSE/AMEX/NASDAQ 1963-2015. Mean annual returns:

Low E/P 1.9% 4.3% 5.2% 9.1%
2 10.1% 12.5% 12.5% 14.4%
3 12.3% 14.5% 15.5% 17.7%
High E/P 14.1% 17.2% 20.1% 27.1%

Key observations:

  • E/P sorts returns powerfully (rows): low E/P earns ~1.9-9.1%, high E/P earns ~14.1-27.1%
  • B/P adds further return variation within E/P classes (columns): for given E/P, high B/P → higher returns
  • The full spread is enormous: 27.1% - 1.9% = 25.2 percentage points
  • B/P is NOT redundant with E/P — it captures additional information about expected returns

Table 2: B/P Predicts Earnings Growth (pp. 14-15)

For given E/P, high B/P firms have HIGHER subsequent earnings growth:

Low B/P 2 3 High B/P
Low E/P High growth ← → Higher growth
High E/P Lower growth ← → Higher growth

This confirms Property (ii): B/P captures expected growth through the conservative accounting channel. High B/P = low ROE = deferred earnings recognition = higher future earnings growth.

Table 3: B/P and Earnings Volatility (pp. 15-16)

For given E/P, high B/P is associated with higher earnings volatility (measured by standard deviation and interdecile range of future earnings changes):

Higher B/P → higher earnings risk for given E/P

This confirms Property (iii): the higher expected growth associated with high B/P comes with higher risk — justifying higher expected returns.

Table 4: Earnings Betas (pp. 16-17)

Earnings betas (sensitivity of firm earnings to aggregate earnings) increase in B/P for given E/P. High B/P firms have higher systematic earnings risk. This provides a fundamental (earnings-based) explanation for the "value premium": it is compensation for bearing systematic earnings risk, not a behavioral anomaly.

Case Studies (pp. 17-20)

Penman & Reggiani illustrate with specific firms:

  • Verizon (high E/P, low B/P, high ROE): stable, low-growth, low risk — "true value"
  • Sanofi (high E/P, high B/P, low ROE): appears cheap but has risky growth — "value trap"
  • Facebook (low E/P, low B/P, very high ROE): high growth priced in, low risk of delivering — "quality growth"
  • Twitter (low E/P, high B/P, low ROE): growth stock with low current profitability — "speculative growth"
  • Coca-Cola (moderate E/P, low B/P, high ROE): franchise value, stable
  • Amazon (low E/P, high B/P, low ROE): massive growth investment depressing current ROE
  • Starbucks (low E/P, low B/P, high ROE): high current profitability with growth priced in

The "Value Trap" Explained (pp. 18-20)

The "value trap" is the phenomenon where "value" stocks (high B/P) underperform despite appearing cheap. Penman & Reggiani explain: these stocks are not cheap — they are bearing growth risk. Their high B/P reflects low ROE from conservative accounting, which in turn reflects growth investments whose payoffs are uncertain. When the growth fails to materialize, the stock collapses. The high average return to "value" investing compensates for this risk of growth failure.

Significance

1. E/P = r - g Vindicates the Earnings Yield as the Core Pricing Variable

Penman & Reggiani's framework starts from E/P = r - g. The earnings yield is the spread between the expected return and expected growth. When g ≈ 0 (mature firms), E/P ≈ r — the earnings yield IS the expected return, exactly as the EPS paradigm claims. Even when g ≠ 0, E/P remains the central pricing variable that determines how investors capitalize earnings.

2. E/P Dominates B/P as the Primary Return Predictor

Table 1 shows that E/P sorts returns more powerfully than B/P. The row spread (E/P) is much larger than the column spread (B/P). This is consistent with Basu (1977, 1983): the earnings yield is the primary cross-sectional predictor of returns. Fama & French's emphasis on B/P (HML) may have the causation backward — E/P is the more fundamental variable, and B/P adds information only about the decomposition of E/P into r and g.

3. The P/E Multiple Reflects Growth Expectations

In the EPS paradigm, the P/E multiple comes from "comps" — comparable firms. Penman & Reggiani's framework reveals what comps are estimating: the growth rate g. High P/E (low E/P) firms have high expected growth, so their earnings are capitalized at a higher multiple. Low P/E (high E/P) firms have low or negative expected growth. The "comps" process is a practical method for estimating g.

4. The "Value Premium" Is an Earnings Yield Premium

The high returns to "value" stocks are not a puzzle in the Penman & Reggiani framework: high B/P stocks have high expected returns because they bear systematic earnings risk. In the EPS paradigm, this translates directly: high earnings yield firms earn high returns because the earnings yield IS the expected return (adjusted for growth). The "value premium" is simply the spread between high and low earnings yields.

5. Conservative Accounting Connects to EPS Paradigm's Use of Reported Earnings

Penman & Reggiani show that conservative accounting creates the link between low ROE and high growth. In the EPS paradigm, managers maximize REPORTED EPS — which is determined by conservative GAAP accounting. The same accounting rules that create the value/growth distinction in Penman & Reggiani's framework are the rules that determine the EPS managers seek to maximize.

6. Book Value Is Secondary — Earnings Are Primary

The framework shows B/P only matters because it captures information about ROE and growth CONDITIONAL on E/P. Strip away B/P, and E/P still predicts returns (the row sort in Table 1). This aligns with the EPS paradigm's focus on earnings rather than book value: earnings capitalization (P = EPS × P/E) is the primary pricing mechanism, and book value enters only as a secondary indicator of earnings quality/persistence.

Paradigm

Practitioner

Earnings capitalization: P = EPS × P/E from comps. Cost of equity = earnings yield. Objective = maximize EPS.

Era

The Great Divergence (1964–present)

Academic textbooks teach NPV/CAPM/DCF. Practitioners continue using EPS × P/E, earnings yields, and comps.

Connections

  • Basu (1977) — Documents the P/E effect (high E/P → high returns). Penman & Reggiani provide the theoretical framework: E/P = r - g, so high E/P → high r (when controlling for or understanding g).
  • Basu (1983) — Shows E/P dominates size in predicting returns. Penman & Reggiani's Table 1 shows E/P dominates B/P in the double sort — consistent across two decades of research.
  • Fama & French (1992, 1993) — FF emphasize B/P (HML) as the value factor. Penman & Reggiani show B/P is secondary to E/P and captures growth/risk conditional on E/P. This challenges the FF framework's focus on B/P rather than E/P.
  • Ohlson (1995) — Ohlson's model with ω = 1 gives P = φx - d (pure earnings capitalization). Penman & Reggiani's E/P = r - g framework is the empirical counterpart, showing how earnings capitalization works in practice with growth firms.
  • Ou & Penman (1989) — Document the Molodovsky/P-E effect: mean-reversion in P/E ratios driven by transitory earnings. Penman & Reggiani extend this by showing how B/P captures the growth that drives P/E dynamics.
  • Lakonishok, Shleifer & Vishny (1994) — "Contrarian Investment, Extrapolation, and Risk." LSV argue the value premium is behavioral (extrapolation bias). Penman & Reggiani argue it is rational compensation for earnings risk. The EPS paradigm is agnostic: the premium exists because E/P is the cost of equity.
  • Ball (1978) — "Anomalies in Relationships Between Securities' Yields and Yield-Surrogates." Ball argues anomalies reflect omitted risk factors. Penman & Reggiani identify the specific risk: systematic earnings volatility associated with growth investments under conservative accounting.
  • Penman (2010) — Accounting for Value. Penman's textbook develops the E/P and B/P framework at length. This paper provides the formal empirical documentation.
  • Gordon (1959) — The constant growth model P = D/(r-g). Penman & Reggiani translate this into earnings terms: P = E/(r-g), giving E/P = r-g.
  • Graham & Dodd (1934) — Graham & Dodd use the earnings yield as the measure of value. Penman & Reggiani show this measure captures r - g, validating Graham & Dodd's use of E/P as a value indicator while clarifying its relation to expected returns.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS) — Penman & Reggiani's E/P = r - g framework formalizes the EPS paradigm's central claim: the earnings yield is the observable market price of equity. When g ≈ 0, E/P = r — the earnings yield IS the cost of equity. Even with growth, E/P captures the core pricing relationship.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS x PE) — The P/E multiple in Penman & Reggiani's framework is 1/(r - g). Investors estimate this from comps — comparable firms with similar growth and risk profiles. This is exactly the EPS × P/E pricing mechanism the investor-side paper describes.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (Equity risk premium) — Penman & Reggiani imply ERP = r - r_f = E/P + g - r_f. For the market as a whole, if growth equals the economy's growth rate, the ERP is approximately the aggregate earnings yield plus GDP growth minus the risk-free rate. In the EPS paradigm's simplest formulation (g ≈ 0 for the representative firm), ERP ≈ E/P - r_f — the observable earnings yield spread.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (Backward-looking AP) — Penman & Reggiani use CURRENT earnings and book value to sort stocks and predict FUTURE returns. This is backward-looking asset pricing: current accounting data determines expected returns. The backward-looking approach works because current earnings are informative about future earnings (Ohlson's ω > 0).
