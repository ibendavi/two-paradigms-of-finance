De Bondt & Thaler (1985): Does the Stock Market Overreact?

De Bondt, Werner F. M., and Richard Thaler. "Does the Stock Market Overreact?" *Journal of Finance* 40, no. 3 (July 1985): 793-805.

Key Finding

De Bondt & Thaler's 1985 JF paper is the first to use a behavioral principle to predict a new market anomaly. Drawing on Kahneman & Tversky's representativeness heuristic — that people overweight recent information when forming expectations — they predict and confirm that the stock market overreacts: extreme past losers outperform extreme past winners by about 25% over the subsequent three years. Specifically, portfolios of prior 3-year "losers" earn cumulative abnormal returns (CARs) of +19.6% over 36 months, while prior "winners" earn CARs of -5.0%, for a difference of 24.6% (t = 2.20). The effect is asymmetric (stronger for losers), concentrated in January, and persists for up to five years. The paper established the "contrarian investing" anomaly in academic finance and, along with Shiller (1981), opened the behavioral challenge to the EMH.

Details

The Behavioral Hypothesis (pp. 793-795)

The paper opens with a direct appeal to psychology:

"Research in experimental psychology has suggested that, in violation of Bayes' rule, most people tend to 'overreact' to unexpected and dramatic news events."

The key behavioral principle is the representativeness heuristic (Kahneman & Tversky [14]; Grether [12]):

  • People overweight recent, salient information
  • They underweight base rates and prior probabilities
  • They extrapolate recent trends too aggressively

The prediction: If investors overreact to recent news, then:

  1. Stocks that have been extreme losers (bad news → overreaction downward) will be underpriced and subsequently earn abnormal positive returns
  2. Stocks that have been extreme winners (good news → overreaction upward) will be overpriced and subsequently earn abnormal negative returns

This is "the first attempt to use a behavioral principle to predict a new market anomaly."

Methodology (pp. 795-797)

  • Data: NYSE stocks, monthly returns from CRSP, January 1926 — December 1982
  • Portfolio formation: Every three years (starting January 1933), rank all stocks by their cumulative abnormal return (CAR) over the prior three years ("formation period")
  • Winner portfolio: Top 35 stocks (highest prior CAR)
  • Loser portfolio: Bottom 35 stocks (lowest prior CAR)
  • Test period: Track the CARs of winner and loser portfolios over the subsequent 36 months
  • Benchmark: Market model residuals (excess returns relative to the market)
  • Replications: 16 non-overlapping three-year test periods (1933-1980)

The Key Results (pp. 797-800)

Table I — Average CARs for Winner and Loser Portfolios (36 months after formation):

  • Loser portfolio CAR: +19.6% (t = 2.20)
  • Winner portfolio CAR: -5.0%
  • Difference (Loser - Winner): 24.6% (t = 2.20)

Figure 1 — The Iconic Chart: The figure shows the CARs of loser and winner portfolios over 36 months post-formation. The loser portfolio CAR rises steadily; the winner portfolio CAR drifts downward. The divergence is dramatic and visually compelling. This became one of the most reproduced figures in behavioral finance.

Asymmetry (pp. 799-800)

The overreaction effect is strongly asymmetric:

  • Loser portfolio: +19.6% (large, significant)
  • Winner portfolio: -5.0% (smaller, less significant)
  • "The overreaction effect is, on average, much larger for losers than for winners"

This asymmetry is consistent with loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky 1979) — negative events trigger stronger psychological reactions than positive ones.

The January Effect (pp. 800-801)

Most of the loser portfolio's excess returns are realized in January:

  • The effect appears in every January of the test period, even the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, and 5th January after formation
  • "The extraordinarily large positive excess returns earned by the loser portfolio every January" remain "without adequate explanation"
  • The January effect is related to but not fully explained by tax-loss selling
  • The winner portfolio loses value at year-end and gains in January — opposite pattern but smaller

Five-Year Formation Period (Figure 3, pp. 802-803)

Using a five-year formation period with 46 yearly replications (1933-1978):

  • Loser portfolio: CARs reach ~35% over 60 months
  • Winner portfolio: CARs fall to ~-15% over 60 months
  • The effect is even larger and persists for the full five years
  • "Much to our surprise, the effect is observed as late as five years after portfolio formation"

Risk Explanation Rejected (pp. 800-801)

Are the losers simply riskier? De Bondt & Thaler examine CAPM betas:

  • The loser portfolio beta is not consistently higher than the winner portfolio beta
  • In fact, by construction, losers tend to be in financial distress with uncertain betas
  • The asymmetry (large loser effect, small winner effect) works against a pure risk explanation
  • "The market portfolio has a beta equal to 1.00 only. This systematic bias may be responsible for the earlier observed asymmetry"

Implications for Other Anomalies (pp. 803-804)

The overreaction hypothesis reinterprets existing anomalies:

  • Size effect: Blume & Stambaugh, Keim, Reinganum — the "small firm" effect may actually be a "losing firm" effect (small firms are often past losers)
  • P/E effect: High P/E stocks are "overvalued" (winners); low P/E stocks are "undervalued" (losers) — the overreaction hypothesis explains both
  • Dividend yield effect: Correlated with P/E, may also reflect overreaction

Significance

1. Behavioral Finance Founded

Along with Shiller (1981) and Kahneman & Tversky (1979), this paper is one of the three founding documents of behavioral finance. It was the first to translate a specific psychological bias into a testable market prediction — and confirm it. This established the methodology of behavioral finance: start with a cognitive bias, derive its market implication, test it empirically.

2. Long-Term Reversal Documented

The paper established the "long-term reversal" anomaly: over 3-5 year horizons, past losers outperform past winners. This was later contrasted with Jegadeesh & Titman's (1993) "short-term momentum" — the market displays short-term continuation (3-12 months) but long-term reversal (3-5 years). This dual pattern remains one of the deepest puzzles in asset pricing.

3. Contrarian Investing Validated

The paper provided rigorous academic evidence for what contrarian practitioners (Graham, Dreman) had long advocated: buy unloved stocks, sell beloved ones. The fact that De Bondt & Thaler cite both Graham and Dreman is significant — this paper explicitly bridges the practitioner and academic streams.

4. The Graham & Dodd Connection

De Bondt & Thaler are among the very few modern academic finance papers to cite Graham. The overreaction hypothesis IS the academic formalization of Graham's "Mr. Market" metaphor — the market oscillates between overoptimism and overpessimism, creating opportunities for patient contrarian investors.

5. Challenge to EMH

The paper posed a fundamental challenge to the efficient markets hypothesis: if markets are efficient, how can a simple strategy of buying past losers earn 25% excess returns over three years? Either the market is not efficient (the behavioral view) or the losers are genuinely riskier (the rational view — later formalized by Fama & French 1993 as the "distress factor"). This debate has never been fully resolved.

Paradigm

Academic

NPV / DCF: P = Σ CF/(1+r)^t. Cost of equity from CAPM / factor model. Objective = maximize firm value.

Era

The Great Divergence (1964–present)

Academic textbooks teach NPV/CAPM/DCF. Practitioners continue using EPS × P/E, earnings yields, and comps.

Connections

  • Built on: Kahneman & Tversky (1979) prospect theory and representativeness; Shiller (1981) excess volatility; Arrow (1982) risk perception
  • Complemented by: Shiller (1981) — excess volatility from the same behavioral forces
