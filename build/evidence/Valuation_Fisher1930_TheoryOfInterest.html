Fisher (1930): The Theory of Interest — The Complete Framework

Fisher, Irving. *The Theory of Interest, as Determined by Impatience to Spend Income and Opportunity to Invest It.* New York: Macmillan, 1930. 620 pages.

Key Finding

Fisher's 1930 revision of his 1907 Rate of Interest is "virtually a new book." The core thesis (capital-value is discounted income) is unchanged, but three major additions transform the work: (1) the "rate of return over cost" — the proto-IRR concept that directly founds the NPV rule for capital budgeting; (2) the Fisher Separation Theorem made fully explicit with geometric proof; and (3) a remarkably early statement of dividend irrelevance — that shareholders can create their own "dividends" by selling shares, and what matters is earning power, not payout. Fisher also cites Edgar Lawrence Smith (1924) on stocks outperforming bonds, connecting the equity premium to his risk framework. This is the book that directly leads to Williams (1938), M&M (1958/1961), and modern corporate finance.

Details

What's New vs. the 1907 Edition

The 1930 edition introduces a "Three Successive Approximations" framework:

  • First Approximation (Chs. III-VI): Time preference/impatience alone (≈ the 1907 book)
  • Second Approximation (Chs. VII-VIII, X-XIII): Investment opportunity added — the major new contribution
  • Third Approximation (Ch. IX): Risk and uncertainty — substantially expanded from 1907

The "Rate of Return Over Cost" — Proto-IRR (Chs. VII-VIII)

The single most important new concept. Fisher defines the "rate of return over cost" as the discount rate that equates the present values of two alternative income streams to zero. In modern terminology: the internal rate of return (IRR) on the incremental investment.

Fisher proves mathematically (Appendix to Ch. XIII, pp. 513-517) that:

Maximizing present value is identical to setting the marginal rate of return over cost equal to the market interest rate: r' = i

This is the theoretical foundation for the NPV rule and the capital budgeting framework that Gerstenberg (1924) and Graham & Dodd (1934) lacked.

Fisher Separation — Now Explicit

Two-step decision, now with full geometric apparatus (Willingness lines + Opportunity lines):

  1. Choose the investment that maximizes present value (at the market rate) — the production/investment decision
  2. Borrow or lend along the market line to achieve desired consumption timing — the financing/consumption decision

This is the theoretical ancestor of M&M (1958): the investment decision is independent of the financing decision.

Dividend Irrelevance Hint (Appendix to Ch. I, p. 508)

Quoting Canning's Economics of Accountancy, Fisher argues:

"Full statement of the earning prospects... would, at least, prevent their urging dividend payments contrary to their own best interests. Full statement, too, even though no dividends are forthcoming, may put the shareholders in a favorable position — through selling part of their holdings or borrowing upon them."

This is a remarkable early statement of dividend irrelevance — shareholders can create "homemade dividends" by selling shares; what matters is the earning power of the enterprise, not the payout. This directly anticipates Miller & Modigliani (1961).

Risk and the Equity Premium (Ch. IX)

The 1930 edition expands risk treatment substantially:

  • Multiple interest rates for different risk classes (riskless → risky)
  • "Coefficients of caution" — risk-averse investors discount expected income more than actuarial probabilities suggest (≈ risk premia)
  • Cites Edgar Lawrence Smith (1924) and Kenneth Van Strum (1925) on stocks outperforming bonds — connecting the equity premium to systematic risk
  • Discusses diversification ("spreading risks") and liquidity as risk mitigation

The Fisher Equation — Now Empirical (Ch. XIX, ~40 pages)

Entirely new chapter: massive statistical analysis of inflation and interest rates across:

  • Great Britain (1820-1924), United States (1890-1927), Berlin, Paris, Calcutta, Tokyo, Shanghai
  • Develops a distributed lag methodology — correlating price changes with interest rates using weighted averages extending 10-20 years back
  • Demonstrates "money illusion": nominal rates fail to adjust fully to inflation
  • This is among the most empirically sophisticated work in economics before 1940

Mathematical Appendices — Extensive

Full calculus proofs absent from 1907:

  • Marginal rate of time preference = interest rate at optimum
  • Rate of return over cost: algebraic expression and optimization condition
  • Present value maximization: complete proof that max PV ⟺ marginal r = i
  • Comparison with Walras and Pareto (neither had investment opportunity framework)

Significance

1. The Missing Link Between Theory and Practice

Fisher (1907) established PV universally but had no capital budgeting rule. Fisher (1930) provides the rate of return over cost — the IRR — and proves it equivalent to NPV maximization. This is what was missing from Dewing (1920) and Gerstenberg (1924): a rigorous basis for investment decisions.

2. Direct Ancestor of Williams (1938)

Williams was a Harvard PhD student in the 1930s. Fisher 1930 provides:

  • PV as the unifying valuation framework → Williams' DDM
  • The investor's required return as the discount rate → Williams' "personal rate"
  • The dividend irrelevance hint → Williams' "dividends, not earnings" argument
  • The risk premium framework → Williams' time-varying discount rates

3. Anticipates M&M Twice

  • Fisher Separation (1930) → M&M Proposition I (1958): investment/production decisions are independent of financing decisions
  • Dividend irrelevance hint (1930, p. 508) → Miller & Modigliani dividend irrelevance (1961)

4. Founds Capital Budgeting

The rate-of-return-over-cost = IRR is the conceptual foundation for:

  • Dean (1951) Capital Budgeting
  • Lorie & Savage (1955) "Three Problems in Rationing Capital"
  • The entire NPV/IRR framework used in modern corporate finance

5. The Present-Value Gap — Finally Closing

  • Fisher (1907): Published, ignored by practitioners
  • Fisher (1930): Still largely ignored by practitioners (Dewing 1920, 5 volumes, zero Fisher)
  • But Williams (1938) picked it up from Fisher 1930 → formalized the DDM
  • The gap between theory (Fisher) and practice (Graham & Dodd) persisted through 1940
