Crump (1874): The Theory of Stock Exchange Speculation

Crump, Arthur. *The Theory of Stock Exchange Speculation.* London, 1874.

Key Finding

Crump provides NO formal equity valuation methodology â€” his framework is entirely probabilistic and behavioral. He treats speculation as a negative-sum game for outsiders, using De Morgan's mathematical expectation formula. The book is historically significant for its remarkably early behavioral finance insights (optimism bias, loss aversion, herding) and its detailed description of 1870s London Stock Exchange microstructure.

Details

Framework: Speculation as Gambling

  • Explicit citation of De Morgan's mathematical expectation formula applied to stock market trading
  • The Stock Exchange = "the bank" at a gaming house; the jobber's bid-ask spread ensures the outside speculator's expected value is negative
  • Detailed worked example: speculator in Turkish 5% bonds who enjoys favorable price moves still loses Â£12 9s 6d after commissions and contango

Three Structural Disadvantages of Outside Speculators

  1. Transaction costs ("the turn"): Bid-ask spread + broker commissions + contango charges
  2. Information asymmetry: Professionals have private cipher telegrams, branch offices abroad, access to jobbers' books â€” "political information is known sooner at the Stock Exchange than anywhere else"
  3. Psychological biases: Hold losers too long, refuse moderate profits, natural bull bias

Behavioral Finance Insights (Remarkably Early)

  • Loss aversion: "speculators hold on to a stock, hoping for a recovery when they have made a loss, with such tenacity that they will leave it as a man drops into the sea from a burning ship"
  • Optimism bias: "negative events have less hold on the mind than positive ones"
  • Herding: Public buys on tips after observing price rises, entering at the top
  • Weather effect: "A speculator should have a good aneroid barometer" â€” foggy weather depresses markets, sunshine lifts them (earliest "sunshine effect" observation?)
  • Calendar anomalies: Detailed month-by-month seasonal trading patterns

What's NOT in the Book

  • No intrinsic value concept
  • No present value or DCF
  • No earnings analysis or EPS
  • No systematic valuation framework
  • Dividends mentioned only in passing as price catalysts, not as valuation basis

"Corrective Speculation" as Social Good

  • Legitimate speculation (buying cheap, selling dear with real capital) performs a price-discovery function
  • Distinguished from "time-bargain" speculation (pure gambling on price differences)

Significance

  1. Earliest application of expected value to stock speculation in a practitioner text
  2. Proto-behavioral finance: Catalog of cognitive biases 100+ years before Kahneman & Tversky
  3. Market microstructure: Detailed description of jobber system, contango, backwardation, options
  4. Zero-sum insight: "For every profit made by a speculator, there must be a corresponding loss"
  5. Practitioner stream only: No engagement with academic theory; pure City of London experience
