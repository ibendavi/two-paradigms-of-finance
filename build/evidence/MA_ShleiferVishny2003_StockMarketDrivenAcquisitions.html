Shleifer & Vishny (2003): Stock Market Driven Acquisitions

Shleifer, Andrei, and Robert W. Vishny. "Stock Market Driven Acquisitions." *Journal of Financial Economics* 68, no. 3 (2003): 295-311. (Shleifer at Harvard University, Department of Economics; Vishny at University of Chicago, Graduate School of Business.)

Key Finding

Shleifer & Vishny's 2003 Journal of Financial Economics paper presents a model of mergers and acquisitions driven by stock market misvaluations rather than real synergies or efficiency gains. The fundamental assumption: financial markets are inefficient (some firms are overvalued, others undervalued), but managers are perfectly rational and informed. In this framework, mergers are "a form of arbitrage by rational managers operating in inefficient markets" (p. 2). Overvalued firms (high Q_1) acquire relatively less overvalued targets (low Q) using stock as payment, allowing the bidder's long-term shareholders to increase their claim on real assets before the overvaluation corrects. Undervalued targets are acquired for cash. The model explains three U.S. merger waves: (1) the 1960s conglomerate wave (stock-financed, high-valuation market, the "purpose was to transfer the high multiple of the acquirer to the earnings of the target"), (2) the 1980s bust-up takeovers (cash-financed, low-valuation market, target undervaluation), and (3) the 1990s related acquisitions (stock-financed, high-valuation market). Nine predictions are generated, including that negative long-run bidder returns in stock acquisitions are NOT evidence of value destruction -- returns would have been even MORE negative without the acquisition. For the EPS project, this paper provides an alternative (behavioral) theory of M&A payment method that complements but differs from the EPS accretion theory: Shleifer & Vishny say overvaluation drives stock payment; the EPS paradigm says EPS dilution avoidance drives stock payment. Crucially, however, the paper explicitly acknowledges the P/E multiple transfer mechanism in explaining the 1960s conglomerate wave.

Details

Section 1: Introduction (pp. 1-3 of manuscript)

Three merger waves in U.S. history:

  1. 1960s conglomerates: Different industries, stock payment, high market valuations
  2. 1980s bust-ups: Raiders with bank debt and junk bonds, cash payment, low market valuations
  3. 1990s related acquisitions: Same industry, stock payment, high market valuations

The puzzle for neoclassical theory: The neoclassical view (mergers as efficiency responses to industry shocks) cannot explain aggregate merger waves, doesn't predict payment method, and has inconclusive evidence on whether mergers improve profitability. Long-run returns are negative for stock acquirers but positive for cash acquirers.

The model's premise: Markets are inefficient. Managers are rational. "Mergers in this model are a form of arbitrage by rational managers operating in inefficient markets." This is the OPPOSITE of Roll's (1986) hubris hypothesis (rational markets, irrational managers).

Section 2: The Model (pp. 4-6)

Setup: Two firms (0 and 1) with capital stocks K and K_1, market valuations per unit of capital Q and Q_1 (Q_1 > Q, so firm 1 is more valued). These are NOT efficient valuations -- they reflect sentiment. Long-run fundamental value is q per unit for both.

Perceived synergy: S = valuation per unit of the combined entity. Typically Q < S < Q_1.

S (no-synergy point):* S*(K + K_1) - KQ - K_1Q_1 = 0. For S > S*, the combined short-run return is positive.

No real long-run gains from mergers: By assumption, all capital is worth q in the long run. No synergies, no managerial improvements.

Section 3: The Arithmetics of Returns (pp. 6-10)

Proposition 1 (Short-run): Combined short-run gain = S(K + K_1) - K_1Q_1 - KQ. This can be positive even without real synergies if S is high enough (perceived synergy).

Proposition 2 (Cash acquisitions, long-run): Long-run combined effect = 0. Target gains K(P-q), bidder gains K(q-P). Cash acquisitions only make sense if target is undervalued (P < q).

Proposition 3 (Stock acquisitions, long-run): Target's long-run gain = qK(P/S - 1). Bidder's long-run gain = qK(1 - P/S). Bidder gains in the long run if and only if P < S (paid less than the blended valuation).

Key insight for stock deals: Even if long-run OBSERVED returns are negative (because the bidder was overvalued to begin with), the acquisition still serves long-term shareholders because returns would have been EVEN MORE negative without it. "Negative bidder returns are not evidence of a failure to serve shareholder interests -- conglomerate values would have fallen even more without them" (p. 20).

Section 4: Discussion -- Why Acquire Rather Than Invest? (pp. 11-15)

Three alternative strategies for an overvalued firm:

  1. Acquire another firm for stock (cost P per unit of target capital)
  2. Make greenfield investment financed by equity (cost R per unit of new capital)
  3. Issue equity and hold cash

Acquisition vs. investment: Acquisition preferred when P is low relative to R and S is high relative to T (post-issuance investment valuation). Acquisitions may dominate because they contribute to earnings growth and help justify high valuations -- "As the policies of Cisco and other technology firms illustrate, acquisitions become part of a growth strategy justifying high market multiples in a way that cash accumulation cannot" (p. 13).

Why targets agree: (1) Target managers have shorter horizons (want to sell out, retire); (2) acquirers pay target managers directly (severance, accelerated options, positions in combined firm).

Section 5.1: Cross-Section of Returns (pp. 16-18)

Consistent evidence:

  • Andrade et al. (2001): 3,688 mergers 1973-1998 -- targets gain 23.8%, acquirers lose 3.8%, combined value change insignificant 1.9%
  • Loughran & Vijh (1997): Cash tender offers earn +43% long-run abnormal returns for bidders
  • Rau & Vermaelen (1998): Cash acquirers earn +8.5% long-run excess returns; stock merger acquirers earn -4%
  • Glamour bidders (high P/E) pay more frequently with stock; value bidders outperform glamour in 3 years post-merger

Section 5.2: Merger Waves (pp. 19-22)

1960s conglomerates:

"Discussions of the 1960s acquisitions often argue that the purpose of these combinations was to transfer the high multiple of the acquirer to the earnings of the target. From this angle, the focus on efficiency aspects of conglomerates in most discussions of the 1960s is just window dressing; the fundamental economics have to do with buying hard capital using overvalued shares." (p. 20)

This is an explicit acknowledgment that the conglomerate wave was about P/E MULTIPLE TRANSFER -- exactly what Pringle (1973), Stern (1974), and Andrade (1997) describe as the EPS accretion game.

1980s bust-up takeovers: Low-valuation market, undervalued targets, cash payment, high hostility. Bust-up value exceeded acquisition price. The wave ended not because of antitakeover legislation but because rising stock prices eliminated undervaluation.

1990s acquisitions: High-valuation market, stock payment, related acquisitions. AOL-Time Warner as the paradigmatic case: "the central feature of this acquisition is not technological synergies, but rather the attempt by the management of overvalued AOL to buy the hard assets of Time Warner to avoid even worse returns in the long run" (p. 22).

Section 5.3: Untested Predictions (p. 23)

Three distinctive predictions:

  1. Cash targets are undervalued in ABSOLUTE terms; stock targets are undervalued RELATIVE to bidders
  2. Bidders in stock acquisitions should exhibit overvaluation signs (insider sales, earnings manipulation, negative post-formation returns)
  3. Bidders have longer horizons than targets, OR pay off target managers

Section 6: Conclusion (pp. 23-24)

"Our model takes mispricing as given. But it also points to a powerful incentive for firms to get their equity overvalued, so that they can make acquisitions with stock... firms with overvalued equity might be able to make acquisitions, survive, and grow, while firms with undervalued, or relatively less overvalued, equity become takeover targets themselves."

The model creates incentives for earnings manipulation (to raise valuations) and thus for the EPS management documented in the empirical literature.

Significance

1. Explicitly Acknowledges P/E Multiple Transfer in 1960s Conglomerates

The quote about the 1960s -- "the purpose of these combinations was to transfer the high multiple of the acquirer to the earnings of the target" -- is a direct statement of the EPS accretion mechanism. Shleifer & Vishny frame this as "overvaluation," but the mechanism they describe IS EPS accretion: high-P/E firm buys low-P/E target, applies its multiple to the target's earnings, and EPS increases.

2. Alternative Theory of M&A Payment Method

The EPS paradigm says payment method is driven by EPS accretion/dilution: stock if EPS-accretive, cash if stock would be dilutive. Shleifer & Vishny say payment method is driven by misvaluation: stock when overvalued, cash when undervalued. These theories overlap but are not identical. High P/E often correlates with overvaluation, so both models predict stock payment by high-P/E firms. But the mechanisms differ: EPS accretion is about the numerator (boosting EPS), while the misvaluation theory is about exploiting mispriced equity.

3. The Cisco/Growth-Strategy Quote Connects to EPS

The observation that "acquisitions become part of a growth strategy justifying high market multiples" (p. 13) is directly about maintaining P/E ratios through earnings growth. Cisco's acquisition strategy in the 1990s was famously driven by EPS accretion -- each acquisition added to earnings, which at Cisco's high P/E, added proportionally more to market capitalization. Shleifer & Vishny describe this as overvaluation maintenance; the EPS paradigm describes it as EPS maximization. Same behavior, different framing.

4. Negative Long-Run Returns Are Consistent with BOTH Theories

Shleifer & Vishny's insight that negative long-run bidder returns in stock deals are not evidence of value destruction -- because returns would have been worse without the acquisition -- is novel. For the EPS paradigm, negative long-run returns are irrelevant: the firm was maximizing EPS, not long-run returns. Both theories predict stock acquisitions during high-P/E periods and negative subsequent returns, but for different reasons.

5. The Earnings Manipulation Incentive

Shleifer & Vishny note that their model "points to a powerful incentive for firms to get their equity overvalued, so that they can make acquisitions with stock" (p. 24). This implies earnings manipulation to raise valuations -- connecting to the entire earnings management literature (Graham, Harvey & Rajgopal 2005; Roychowdhury 2006). In the EPS paradigm, earnings manipulation is directly about EPS targets; in the misvaluation model, it's instrumentally about raising market valuation to enable stock acquisitions.

6. The "Perceived Synergy" S as a Market Narrative

The parameter S (perceived synergy) is described as "the story that the market consensus holds about the benefits of the merger" -- it "might be invented by investment bankers or academics and have little to do with the reality of what drives actual acquisitions" (p. 5). This framing aligns with the EPS paradigm's view that the P/E multiple incorporates market narratives, not just fundamental value. The synergy "story" is what allows the market to assign a high S to the combined entity.

7. Behavioral vs. EPS Paradigm Framework

Shleifer & Vishny operate within the behavioral corporate finance framework (inefficient markets, rational managers exploiting mispricing). The EPS paradigm does NOT require market inefficiency -- EPS maximization operates whether or not stocks are correctly priced. This is a meaningful theoretical distinction: the EPS paradigm's predictions hold in efficient markets (where EPS accretion is still the mechanism), while Shleifer & Vishny's require mispricing.

Paradigm

Practitioner

Earnings capitalization: P = EPS × P/E from comps. Cost of equity = earnings yield. Objective = maximize EPS.

Era

The Great Divergence (1964–present)

Academic textbooks teach NPV/CAPM/DCF. Practitioners continue using EPS × P/E, earnings yields, and comps.

Connections

  • Andrade (1997) -- "Do Appearances Matter?" Tests EPS accretion as M&A driver. Shleifer & Vishny's model provides an alternative explanation (misvaluation) for the same patterns. Andrade's finding that stock deals are more likely when they're EPS-accretive is consistent with both theories (high P/E implies both overvaluation and EPS accretion potential).
  • Dasgupta, Harford & Ma (2024) -- "EPS Sensitivity and Merger Deals." Show deals are structured around EPS accretion/dilution thresholds. Shleifer & Vishny would interpret this as acquirers who happen to be overvalued (high P/E, where stock deals are mechanically EPS-accretive) using stock.
  • Pringle (1973) -- "Price Earnings Ratios, Earnings-Per-Share, and Financial Management." Describes the P/E-EPS view of M&A as practitioners using high P/E to "acquire earning power." Shleifer & Vishny explicitly reference this mechanism for the 1960s conglomerates.
  • Stern (1974) -- "Earnings per Share Don't Count." The AB-BA Fallacy in M&A. Shleifer & Vishny's model would say: it's not EPS accretion that matters, it's relative overvaluation. But the behavioral prediction (high-P/E firm acquires low-P/E target) is the same.
  • Baker & Wurgler (2002) -- "Market Timing and Capital Structure." Shleifer & Vishny cite them as fellow travelers in the behavioral corporate finance framework. Baker & Wurgler show equity issuance occurs when market-to-book is high; Shleifer & Vishny extend this to M&A.
  • Jensen (1986) -- "Agency Cost of Free Cash Flow." Provides the "synergy story" for the 1980s bust-up wave that Shleifer & Vishny's model requires for S > S*. In Shleifer & Vishny's framework, Jensen's free cash flow theory is just the narrative that enables the takeover, not the fundamental driver.
  • Roll (1986) -- "The Hubris Hypothesis of Corporate Takeovers." The exact opposite of Shleifer & Vishny: Roll assumes efficient markets and irrational managers. Shleifer & Vishny assume inefficient markets and rational managers. The EPS paradigm is different from both: it assumes managers rationally maximize EPS regardless of market efficiency.
  • Myers & Majluf (1984) -- "Corporate Financing and Investment Decisions." In pecking order theory, stock issuance signals overvaluation. Shleifer & Vishny's model assumes markets DON'T learn from the stock issuance decision, unlike Myers-Majluf.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (EPS) -- The EPS paradigm and the misvaluation theory make overlapping but distinguishable predictions about M&A payment method. Both predict stock payment by high-P/E firms, but for different reasons: EPS accretion vs. exploiting overvaluation. The key empirical distinction: do firms choose payment method based on the EPS accretion condition (E/P vs. r_d) or based on misvaluation measures (book-to-market, prior returns)? Dasgupta et al. (2024) provide evidence for the EPS channel.
  • Ben-David & Chinco (maxEPS paradigm) -- Shleifer & Vishny's explicit acknowledgment that the 1960s conglomerates were about "transferring the high multiple of the acquirer to the earnings of the target" places even these leading behavioral finance theorists within the EPS framework. They describe the mechanism but attribute it to overvaluation rather than EPS maximization as an objective function.
  • Ben-David & Chinco, Meli (EPS and financial markets) -- Shleifer & Vishny's prediction that merger waves coincide with high market valuations parallels the Meli paper's argument that falling rates create EPS accretion opportunities. Both predict that stock-for-stock M&A increases in high-valuation environments, but the EPS mechanism operates through the earnings yield vs. borrowing cost gap while the misvaluation mechanism operates through Q ratios.
