Black & Scholes (1973): The Pricing of Options and Corporate Liabilities

Black, Fischer, and Myron Scholes. "The Pricing of Options and Corporate Liabilities." *Journal of Political Economy* 81, no. 3 (May-June 1973): 637-654.

Key Finding

Black & Scholes's 1973 JPE paper derives a closed-form formula for option pricing using a no-arbitrage hedging argument. The core insight: a continuously rebalanced hedge (long stock + short options) eliminates ALL risk, so the hedged position must earn the risk-free rate. This yields a differential equation whose solution is the Black-Scholes formula: w = xN(d‚ÇÅ) - ce^{r(t-t)}N(d‚ÇÇ)*. The formula's most remarkable property: the expected return on the stock does NOT appear ‚Äî the option value depends only on the stock price, exercise price, time to maturity, risk-free rate, and variance. The paper extends the formula to puts (via put-call parity), warrants, and ‚Äî most profoundly ‚Äî to viewing equity as a call option on the firm's assets and bonds as risk-free debt minus a put option on the firm's assets. This unifies option pricing, corporate finance, and the theory of the firm. The paper launched the modern derivatives industry and, with M&M (1958) and Ross (1976), established no-arbitrage as the central methodology of financial economics.

The differential equation (7) is identified as "the heat-transfer equation of physics" (p. 644), solved via Churchill (1963).

The Stunning Absence of Expected Return (p. 644)

"Note that the expected return on the stock does not appear in equation (13). The option value as a function of the stock price is independent of the expected return on the stock."

This is the paper's deepest result. Because the hedge eliminates risk, the expected return is irrelevant ‚Äî only variance matters. This is why options can be priced without knowing whether the stock will go up or down on average.

Corporate Liabilities as Options (pp. 649-652)

The paper's most profound extension ‚Äî viewing the firm through the lens of option theory:

Equity as a call option:

  • Consider a firm with assets (value x), bonds (face value c, maturity t*), and stock
  • At maturity: stockholders receive max(x-c, 0) ‚Äî exactly a call option on firm assets
  • Stock value = w(x,t) from equation (13), where v¬≤ = variance of firm asset returns

Bonds as risk-free debt minus a put:

  • Bond value = x - w(x,t) = firm assets minus call option
  • Equivalently: bond value = ce^{r(t-t*)} - [put option on firm assets]
  • The default discount on bonds = value of the put option = probability-weighted cost of default

This yields a formula for the credit spread on risky bonds ‚Äî purely from option theory, with no need for credit rating models.

Capital structure implications (footnote 12, p. 650):

"The fact that the total value of a corporation is not affected by its capital structure, in the absence of taxes and other imperfections, was first shown by Modigliani and Miller (1958)."

M&M's capital structure irrelevance is EMBEDDED in the option framework: total firm value x is fixed, and the formula merely divides it between equity (call) and debt (firm minus call).

Dividend Policy and Capital Structure (pp. 650-651)

  • Increasing debt (raising c) ‚Üí bond prices fall, stock prices rise (stockholders benefit at bondholders' expense)
  • Increasing dividends ‚Üí stock price rises, bond price falls (stockholders "steal" from bondholders)
  • Bond indentures restrict dividends precisely because of this option-theoretic incentive ‚Üí directly connects to Jensen & Meckling (1976) agency costs of debt

Empirical Tests (p. 653)

  • Tested on call-option data (Black & Scholes 1972)
  • Systematic deviations: option buyers pay more than formula predicts; option writers receive approximately formula value
  • Difference attributed to transaction costs effectively paid by buyers
  • Market underestimates effect of variance differences (options on high-risk stocks underpriced by formula relative to market)

The Treynor Credit (p. 637, footnote)

"The inspiration for this work was provided by Jack L. Treynor (1961a, 1961b)."

Treynor's unpublished memoranda on asset pricing also inspired Sharpe's CAPM. Black & Scholes also thank "Eugene F. Fama, Robert C. Merton, and Merton H. Miller" for comments.

Significance

1. The No-Arbitrage Pricing Revolution ‚Äî Completed

M&M (1958) introduced no-arbitrage reasoning for capital structure. Black-Scholes applied it to derivatives pricing with continuous hedging. Ross (1976) would apply it to asset pricing generally. Together, these three papers established no-arbitrage as the central methodology of financial economics, displacing the older optimization-based approach (Markowitz ‚Üí CAPM).

2. Expected Returns Don't Matter for Pricing

The formula's independence from expected returns is philosophically profound: you don't need to know whether a stock is going up or down to price its options. Only the volatility (variance) matters. This "preference-free" pricing ‚Äî where risk preferences cancel out ‚Äî would later be generalized as "risk-neutral pricing."

3. Corporate Liabilities as Options

The insight that equity = call option on firm assets and debt = risk-free debt minus put transformed corporate finance:

  • Credit risk modeling: Merton (1974) structural model of default builds directly on Black-Scholes
  • Capital structure: agency conflicts between debt and equity arise BECAUSE equity is an option (convex payoff ‚Üí incentive for risk-shifting) ‚Äî connects to Jensen & Meckling (1976)
  • Convertible bonds, warrants, callable debt: all analyzed as packages of options

4. The Derivatives Industry Created

Before Black-Scholes: options were priced by intuition and rough rules. After: a mathematical formula that could be programmed into calculators and computers. The CBOE opened in April 1973, the same month the paper was published. Within a decade, Black-Scholes was the standard pricing model on trading floors worldwide. Scholes received the Nobel Prize in 1997 (Black had died in 1995).

5. The Assumptions and Their Limitations

The model assumes constant volatility, continuous trading, no transaction costs, and log-normal returns. All are violated in practice:

  • Volatility is NOT constant ‚Üí "volatility smile/skew"
  • Trading is discrete ‚Üí hedging error
  • Transaction costs exist ‚Üí delta-hedging is imperfect
  • Fat tails (Fama 1965, Mandelbrot 1963) ‚Üí underpricing of tail risk
  • The 1987 crash and 2008 crisis exposed the real-world costs of these assumptions
  • Yet the model remains the benchmark ‚Äî deviations are measured FROM Black-Scholes, not from alternatives
