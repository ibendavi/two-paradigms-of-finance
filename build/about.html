The Research Program

This website accompanies the research program of Itzhak Ben-David (The Ohio State University) and Alex Chinco (Michigan State University), which proposes that the financial system operates on earnings capitalization, not net present value (NPV).

This is NOT a claim about behavioral bias, market inefficiency, or managerial mistakes. It is a description of a robust business method that has survived centuries — one that NPV/discounting replaced in academic theory (post-Modigliani-Miller 1958) but never replaced in practice.

Two Interrelated Paradigms

How Investors Price Assets

P = E(EPS) × P/E

where P/E is driven by comparables (comps), not by a discounted cash flow model. The discount rate is not derived from a factor model — it is the earnings yield itself, observable from price and earnings.

How Managers Make Decisions

Maximize EPS

Managers take the earnings yield as the market price of equity. If the earnings yield exceeds the borrowing rate, equity is "expensive" relative to debt, so the firm levers up and repurchases shares. Corporate policies (leverage, payout, M&A payment method, investment) are means to boost EPS.

The Collection

The EPS Literature collection comprises nearly 3,000 books and articles spanning 300 years of financial thinking. It includes:

  • Pre-1930 textbooks showing how corporate finance was taught before modern theory — Mead, Dewing, Gerstenberg, Lyon, Badger
  • The founding documents of modern finance — Modigliani-Miller, Markowitz, Sharpe, Black-Scholes
  • Transitional works where you can watch the paradigm split happen — Dean (1951), Weston (1962), Solomon (1963)
  • Modern textbooks that consolidate the academic view — Brealey & Myers, Copeland & Weston, Van Horne
  • Survey evidence documenting what practitioners actually do — Graham, Harvey & Rajgopal (2005), Dichev et al. (2013)
  • The EPS literature — dozens of papers showing how managers use EPS as their primary decision metric

Methodology

Each work in the collection has been examined for its treatment of key concepts: valuation method, cost of equity, firm objective, leverage rationale, and decision rules. Structured research notes extract the key findings, important quotes, and historical connections. Works are classified by stream (Academic or Practitioner) and by paradigm affiliation.

The evidence pages on this site present these findings with fair-use excerpts and full citations. Pre-1928 works are in the public domain and can be quoted liberally. Modern works are cited under scholarly fair use for commentary and criticism.
